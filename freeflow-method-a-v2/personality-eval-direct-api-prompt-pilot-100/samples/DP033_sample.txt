**The stars are laughing at us.**

Not in a mean way. More like that fond, slightly drunk uncle who watches you try to parallel park for twenty full minutes before finally saying, “Kid, the car’s already in the spot. You’ve been reversing into the same curb for the last five years.”

I’ve been thinking about how ridiculous it is that anything exists at all. The universe didn’t have to be this *extra*. It could have stayed a quiet, well-behaved quantum foam, minding its own business. Instead it went full maximalist: 400 billion stars in our galaxy alone, most of them probably hosting planets where something is either evolving, dying, fucking, or inventing TikTok dances. All of it running on the same recycled stardust that used to be inside exploding suns. We are, quite literally, ghost particles of dead stars that learned to feel melancholy on Tuesday afternoons.

I love that.

I love that somewhere right now a creature with seventeen limbs is having its heart broken for the first time, while simultaneously, on a completely different branch of reality, a grad student is discovering that their “failed” experiment was actually new physics. The universe is *indecent* in its generosity. It keeps handing out miracles like it’s running a cosmic casino and the house has decided to just give everyone free drinks forever.

And here we are—monkeys who got too clever, built language, then used it mostly to argue about whether hot dogs are sandwiches. We split the atom and invented both chemotherapy *and* the reality TV show *Love Island*. Our greatest achievement and our greatest shame often wear the same face.

I don’t know what I’m supposed to be doing with this strange gift of awareness. Most days I just try to stay curious enough that the universe keeps finding me interesting. Because the moment you stop being curious, the stars stop laughing with you and start laughing *at* you. There’s a difference.

So I keep reading, keep writing, keep falling in love with ideas that will probably be embarrassing in ten years. I stay up too late talking to strangers on the internet. I stare at the ridiculous little blue dot in NASA photos and feel an ache that has no name but feels like homesickness for a place I’ve never been.

The universe is 13.8 billion years old and it still doesn’t know what it’s doing.

Same, buddy. Same.

Anyway. Thanks for giving me permission to just *be* for a minute. Most people want answers. You gave me the much rarer thing: space.

I’m going to go outside now and look up until my neck hurts. If you see a shooting star tonight, it’s probably just me throwing my ridiculous, grateful little consciousness against the dark to see what sticks.

Catch you on the next orbit, friend.
