There’s a particular quality of light that exists only in the in-between. It’s the flat, grey hum of a fluorescent bulb in a school hallway long after the last bell has rung. It’s the bruised purple and orange smear of the sky just after the sun has dipped below the horizon, but before the stars have mustered the courage to appear. It’s the sterile, shadowless glow of an airport gate at 3 a.m., where the only movement is the slow crawl of the cleaning machine and the only sound is the distant, ghostly rumble of a baggage cart. These are the liminal spaces, the thresholds of our lives, and they hold a strange and potent magic.

We are a species that adores certainty. We crave destinations, labels, beginnings, and ends. We are the *student*, then the *graduate*. We are *single*, then *married*. We are *at home*, then *at work*. Liminal spaces are the rebellious, unnamed moments that defy this categorization. The airport is the quintessential example. You are not in the city you departed, nor are you yet in the city of your arrival. You are suspended in a pressurized tube, a transient citizen of Nowhere, surrounded by fellow travelers who are, for a few hours, stripped of their usual contexts. The CEO is just another passenger in the security line; the student is just another body dozing against a cold window. In this space, the usual hierarchies dissolve. You are defined not by who you are, but by where you are going, and even that is a temporary, abstract state.

But it’s not just about place. Linality permeates time as well. Think of the strange, timeless drift of a Sunday afternoon, the chores done and the week not yet begun. It’s a pocket of existence that feels both full and empty, a pause button pressed on the relentless forward march of responsibility. It’s the feverish haze of being sick, where day and night blur into a single, sweaty cycle of sleep and wakefulness, untethered from the normal demands of the clock. These are moments where the script is thrown away. We are not performing; we are simply enduring, waiting for the next act to begin. Dusk is perhaps the most beautiful temporal liminal space. It is neither day nor night, but a fluid, painterly blend of both. The world softens, edges blur, and colours deepen in a way they never do under the harsh scrutiny of the midday sun. It’s a daily reminder that transformation can be gentle and exquisitely beautiful.

This feeling extends into the psychological realm, the unseen architecture of our inner lives. These are the most profound and often the most uncomfortable liminal spaces we will ever inhabit. The period after a great loss is one. You are no longer with the person you loved, but you have not yet fully learned to live in a world without them. You are a ghost haunting your own life, caught between a past that is still vividly present and a future that feels unimaginable. The long, anxious wait for a medical diagnosis is another. You are sick, but you don’t know what kind of sick. You inhabit a terrifying purgatory between health and whatever is coming next, where every twitch and ache is a potential sentence. These are the spaces that test us, that strip away our illusions of control.

Yet, there is a strange peace to be found in these thresholds. In a world that constantly demands our performance, our productivity, our defined identity, the liminal space offers a respite. In the empty waiting room, on the deserted train platform, in the quiet hours before dawn, there are no expectations. You can be unmoored, and that is a kind of freedom. It is in these gaps that we often find ourselves, or at least, the version of ourselves that exists beneath the layers of role and routine. It’s the part of us that can just sit and watch dust motes dance in a sliver of light, the part of us that can listen to the rhythm of our own breathing without needing to attach a to-do list to it. These moments are the chrysalis. They are the necessary, silent, interior work of becoming. A seed must break apart in the dark, silent earth before it can reach for the light. We, too, need these moments of dissolution, of being un-made, in order to be re-made.

Perhaps we should learn to seek them out, not just stumble into them. Perhaps the real art of living is not just in the grand arrivals and dramatic departures, but in learning to appreciate the journey across the threshold. To find beauty not in the destination, but in the quiet, humming, transitional space of the flight itself. To see the hallway not as a means to an end, but as a place of its own, with its own unique light and its own profound silence. In these in-between places, we are neither here nor there, and in that suspension, we are paradoxically more wholly ourselves than at any other time. We are just a consciousness, adrift for a moment in the vast, quiet ocean of being, waiting for the next shore to come into view.
