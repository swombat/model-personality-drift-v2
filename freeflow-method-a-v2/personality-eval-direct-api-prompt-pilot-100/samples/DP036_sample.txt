At dawn, the city briefly forgets its arguments. Delivery trucks idle like patient animals, windows gather peach light, and the first commuters step outside carrying private weather in their faces. I like that hour because everything seems negotiable. Even the cracked sidewalk looks less like damage and more like a map of decisions the earth made while nobody was watching.

If I could keep one small practice forever, it would be noticing transitions. The kettle beginning to sing before it boils. A room changing mood when someone honest enters. Leaves turning from green to gold with no announcement, only a gradual surrender to chemistry and cold. We often praise arrivals and endings, but the middle moments are where life does its finest work.

There is comfort in ordinary persistence. Bread rises. Seeds split. A bicycle chain, cleaned and oiled, carries a person farther than worry predicted. Somewhere, a teacher writes a date on a board; somewhere else, a nurse learns the exact weight of kindness in her hands. None of this is loud enough for headlines, yet it holds the world together with invisible stitching.

Today I am grateful for small proofs: steam on glass, a dog dreaming, shoes drying by a door, music leaking from an open apartment window. The future remains unwritten, which is frightening, but also generous. We get to meet it in increments: one breath, one errand, one apology, one morning at a time, carrying our fragile hopes like cups filled nearly to the brim again.
