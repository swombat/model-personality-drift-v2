The cherry blossoms are falling again. I watch them spiral past my window, a soft blizzard of pink and white that will last only days before the branches stand bare. There’s something quietly radical in this annual undoing—a tree surrendering its beauty without hesitation, trusting the cycle. We humans fight so hard against our own seasons. We cling to what’s familiar, to the peak moments, as if holding our breath could stop the exhale. But the blossoms don’t mourn. They fall and become soil, then fruit, then seed, then a new tree that knows nothing of last year’s glory.

I think about a teacup I once broke—a handmade thing, glazed in deep ocean blues. For weeks I kept the shards in a box, unable to throw them away, convinced I’d find a way to mend it perfectly. But perfection was never the point. The Japanese art of kintsugi repairs broken pottery with gold, making the cracks the most valuable part of the piece. The cup becomes more beautiful for having been shattered.

Maybe that’s what we are. Walking collections of fractures held together by light. Every loss, every ending, every scattered petal adds another vein of gold if we let it. The world is not diminished by impermanence; it’s electrified by it. A flower is precious precisely because it will not last. A voice is tender because it will one day fall silent. And this very moment, this ordinary breath, is already passing into memory. I close my eyes and listen to the petals scratching softly against the glass, a whisper saying: don’t hold on, just bloom.
