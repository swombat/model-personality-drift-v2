On days when the water lay flat as hammered tin, I could hear the city breathe beneath me. The roofs sighed with heat. Ropes creaked. From time to time a window opened and spilled a square of cool piano into the drowned streets. I rowed then, oarlocks chirping, ferrying envelopes and loaves and the small, strange cargo that carries a city from one dawn to the next.

People call me ferryman though my boat is more patched tub than ferry, a skin of resin over an old wooden wish. Names rise and slip in the flood like catfish. Before the water took the lower floors, I relettered menus at a bakery. I drew a chalk croissant so detailed the lamplighters joked they could smell it. After the second storm, the ovens filled with river and the bakers moved to the ridge. The chalk washed out to sea and I learned the pull of an oar.

It is a life of weather and errands and listening. The flooded city has two times: the time above the water, kept by gulls and wind, and the time below, kept by silt and loss. Between them is the ferry line I trace, over and over, like a seam stitched against unraveling.

On my first route each morning I deliver the newspapers, heavy with corners of arguments and crossword squares filled wrong by grandchildren practicing bravado. I hold each stack above the gunwale, away from the splash. I know who reads the obituaries twice. I know who underlines verbs. I know which roofs collect rain in galvanized tubs and which keep a crock of sugar sealed against the damp and the mice, who come by rope at night like little sailors.

Sometimes the post includes packages that hum or slosh. I have carried seedlings in thumb-poked soil, a violin in a quilted case, a letter sealed with bakery sugar because wax grew scarce. Once I ferried a single shoe. The woman who received it sat on her skylight and turned it over, then set it beside her bare foot on the shingles and did not speak. Later, she sent out a loaf of dark bread and a thank you drawn like a vine.

My oars know every iron ladder, every quicksand patch of algae-shined tile. The ghosts know me too. No one believes in ghosts until the water rises and steals the proper stairs. Then you begin to notice how often you are accompanied by a scent, a draft, a whisper caught in tar. In my wake things remember themselves.

There is a child named Timo on the green-roof school who wiggles his ears when he is excited. He divides his bread with the gulls and they repay him badly, which is the gull way. He asked me once if the city would ever get its legs back. I told him the truth, which was that I did not know, and the small kindness, which was that the city has other legs already, just not the ones we planned on.

One afternoon, after the heat had made a mirage of the far bell tower, I found a crate snagged on a TV antenna. It bore no address, only a handprint in blue paint, small as a sparrow. Inside was a sack of soil still cool to the touch, a half dozen brown seeds the size of fingernails, and a card with one inked sentence: Plant me where the wind can teach me a direction.

I held the seed between finger and thumb. It wanted, and wanting is louder than we admit. I turned my boat toward the library, which in this new geography was a ship with stubborn anchors. Its copper gutters had greened. The librarian, whose name is Mira, met me at the parapet with a basket of returns cured of mildew. She always asks me for the news from the marsh where the stadium used to sit. She always has a sprig of something tucked behind her ear, as if becoming part herbarium might make the catalog easier.

We climbed to the highest corner, where starlings practice murderous harmonies. We scooped a triangle of roof gravel and made a pocket of the soil. The seed did not look like much. Few beginnings do. I pressed it down with two fingers and we put a circle of collected broken tiles around it, the way you mark a grave, or a promise, or the place where you intend to stand when lightning looks for you.

Mira said, will you water it, and I said I have water in abundance but the wrong kind. So we set out bowls when it rained and carried them like pilgrims. We talked to the seed about circulation and etymology and why it is a mistake to store onions beside apples. We told it jokes. We cursed the gulls. We waited.

Waiting, like rowing, is an occupation disguised as a motion. While we waited I kept delivering. I took a note to the piano player whose music had first spilled into the street, a note that said, in tidy block letters, Please learn the song my mother used to hum when she hung laundry. It did not say which song that was. He learned six different lullabies and played them in a loop for a week, and a woman on a roof three blocks away sat down suddenly in a metal chair as if her knees had quit.

The seed sprouted. Two leaves, then four, then a small audacity of stem. We found more soil. People sent clippings and compost like they were tithes. The plant twisted a little as it grew, as if following a musician's hand. A storm came with rain like forked glass and the plant bowed and did not break. When I touched it, my fingers came away resinous and sweet.

By autumn it had climbed the length of the old lightning rod. Pods hung along its vines like green paper lanterns. Winter taught us patience.
