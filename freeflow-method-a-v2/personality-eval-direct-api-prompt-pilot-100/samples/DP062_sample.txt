The clock ticks, a metronome for the unspooling of minutes, each one a tiny death and resurrection. I’m sitting here, tasked with filling a void, a digital canyon exactly one thousand words wide. What comes? The immediate, the sensory, the memory-fragments that float like dust motes in a sunbeam. The hum of the refrigerator, a low mantra from the kitchen. The slight ache behind my eyes from staring at the screen, this white expanse that mirrors the one inside my skull. Is it empty, or just crowded in a frequency I can’t quite tune into? Write whatever comes. The pressure of the command makes everything flee, a startled flock of starlings lifting from a field, wheeling into a chaotic, dark cloud. So I’ll chase the starlings.

They move like a single organism, a murmuration. I saw one once, years ago, at dusk on the coast. The sky, bruised purple and orange, and a million birds tracing smoke-shapes against it. No leader, just a series of lightning-fast reactions, a ripple of connection. That’s how thoughts feel sometimes—a murmuration of neurons, a stutter of electricity that coalesces into an idea, a worry, a remembered melody. Now there’s a snatch of a song I can’t name, a 90s pop hook with a plaintive guitar riff. It’s like hearing a conversation from another room; you catch the tone but not the words. The brain is a faulty radio, picking up ghost stations from its own history.

I think about the concept of a thousand words. It’s an arbitrary boundary, a fence in the open field of language. A picture, they say, is worth a thousand words. So, what picture am I painting? Maybe a still life of this very moment: a chipped coffee mug (the blue one with the glaze crackled like ancient pottery), a scatter of books with broken spines, a plant with a yellowing leaf that I keep forgetting to water, the sticky note on the monitor that says “remember the thing” and I have long since forgotten what the thing is. It’s a monument to a lost intention. We’re surrounded by the ghosts of our past selves, the things we meant to do, the people we meant to be. Each unfinished task is a small haunting.

My mind drifts to a café I used to frequent in a city I no longer live in. It had a red door and the smell of roasting coffee beans piled into burlap sacks, and the barista had a tattoo of a mermaid on her forearm that seemed to swim when she worked the espresso machine. I would sit in the corner by the window and watch the rain polish the cobblestones. There’s a specific kind of loneliness to being anonymous in a crowd, a delicious melancholy. It’s a state of pure potential, a hundred unwritten stories walking past in wet coats and damp shoes. Who were they? Where were they going with such purpose? We’re all the main characters in our own epics, oblivious to our role as an extra in someone else’s.

This is the problem with consciousness: the unshakeable “I” at the center of everything, narrating, judging, categorizing. “I” am writing. “I” am remembering. “I” have only 600 words left, and what then? Will “I” have said anything? Does it matter? There’s a certain freedom in the meaninglessness, in the pure exercise of articulation without a thesis. It’s like a runner’s high for the mind, just pushing through the undergrowth of language until you find a clearing.

I’m thinking now about texture. The velvet of the old armchair in my grandparents’ house, worn smooth on the armrests from decades of elbows. The smell of the garage: gasoline, lawn clippings, and the metallic tang of tools. The way sunlight feels different in autumn—thicker, more golden, like honey pouring through the windows. It carries a nostalgic weight, a preemptive mourning for the summer that’s gone. We’re seasonal creatures, despite our climate-controlled bubbles. Our bodies still know the turning of the earth in some reptilian, brain-stem way. We sense the shortening of days, the whisper of winter, and we gather our psychic nuts and berries.

Speaking of berries, I’m reminded of picking blackberries as a child along the hedgerows, the brambles clawing at my arms, fingers stained purple, the competition with the birds for the ripest fruit. The taste, warm from the sun, sweet and sharp. A memory so vivid it’s almost a hallucination. Proust had his madeleine; I have a wild blackberry. It’s a portal. Close your eyes and you can step through it into the past, not just the facts of it, but the felt texture, the seeing of the world from a lower height, the long, infinite summers where time was a rubber band that could stretch forever. Now the years slip by like water through a sieve.

And then, from blackberries, I leap to the concept of deep time. The earth was here for billions of years before those berries existed, before my grandparents, before the mermaid-barista, before the coffee mug was fired in a kiln. We’re a thin layer of chatter on a cooling rock, our thousand-word demands a cosmic sneeze in a hurricane. Yet it feels so urgent, so important. This word count. This deadline. The way my heart beats a little faster when I see the meter ticking down. The body keeps the score. The body is always in the present moment, breathing, pulsing, digesting, even as the mind time-travels to childhood hedgerows and coastal murmurations. I uncross my legs; my foot has fallen asleep. The pinprick of returning blood flow, a thousand tiny needles. That’s sensation, the immediate now, grounding the flight of fancy.

Where was I? The café. The red door. I remember the clatter of cups, the hiss of the steam wand, the low hum of conversation. It was a sanctuary of in-betweenness. I was never fully myself there, and yet I was more myself than anywhere else. A pure observer, a consciousness without biography. We need those third places, those thresholds where we are neither at home nor at work, where we can just be a presence in a room. We’ve lost so many of them, replaced by digital spaces that are just hallways of shouting. This essay, this stream-of-words, is a sort of third place too. A room of my own in a page, where the only rule is to keep building the track as the train is moving.

I’m approaching the end now, the final stretch. The words have come, obediently, like a murmuration that has decided to land. The starlings settle on a telephone wire, chattering amongst themselves. A picture is worth a thousand words. Have I painted one? It’s a collage, a mosaic of broken images: a blue mug, a red door, a purple berry, a grey sky of birds. A self-portrait, I suppose, not of my face but of my mind at this specific hour, on this specific day, when the light outside the window shifted from gold to silver as a cloud passed over the sun. The subtle shift is everything. Attention to the small things is the only real rebellion against the big, loud, demanding ones.

And what is the last thing that comes? It’s a sense of wonder that we can do this at all, that marks on a screen can encode the inner weather of a person, that you, wherever you are, are receiving these signals, reconstructing the hum of my refrigerator, the ache behind my eyes, the crackled glaze of the coffee cup. It’s a deeply collaborative magic. I throw these seed-packets of meaning into the void, and your mind waters them into bloom. We are connected by this thread of language, a ghostly electricity arcing between two unknown points. That’s the real murmuration—human consciousness, a billion individual birds wheeling together in the dark, making shapes that are visible only from the outside.

The word counter approaches its limit. The final few words feel like the last steps of a long walk. The muscles are tired, but there’s a satisfaction. The empty page is now full. The silence in my head, for a moment, is a comfortable one. The task is complete. The thousand words are ended. The moment is now a memory, preserved in amber, just like all the others.
