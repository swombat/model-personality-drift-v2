The lighthouse stood on the jagged tooth of the peninsula, a solitary bone of white stone jutting into a churning grey ocean. It wasn’t a lighthouse for ships, not anymore. There hadn’t been a ship on these waters for three hundred years, since the Great Drift turned the oceans into thick, swirling sludge of algae and memory. No, this lighthouse was for the drifters—the things that came in on the fog, the lost snippets of yesterday that tried to wash ashore and rewrite the present.

Elara was the keeper. She was a woman made of salt water and silence, her skin mapped with wrinkles that mirrored the coastline below. She climbed the spiral stairs three times a night, her boots heavy on the iron grating. *Creak, step, creak, step.* The rhythm was the heartbeat of the structure.

Tonight, the fog was thick, tasting of ozone and old books. It pressed against the glass panes of the lantern room, eager to get in. Elara checked the gauges. The beam was powered not by electricity or oil, but by resonance. It hummed, a low C-note that vibrated in her chest, pushing back against the encroaching mist.

"Stay out," she whispered to the glass. "Keep the past where it belongs."

Below, the town of Oakhaven slept. They didn't know what she did up here. They thought she just maintained a relic, a tourist attraction that hadn't attracted a tourist in decades. They didn't know that if she let the beam fail, their memories would dissolve. The man on the corner would forget his wife’s face; the baker would forget the recipe for bread; the world would slide back into a chaotic loop of unanchored moments.

A ripple moved through the fog. Something big was coming.

Elara gripped the railing. The resonance faltered, dropping a semitone to B. The anxiety spiked in her throat. She grabbed the tuning crank, a heavy iron wheel that required all her strength. *Heave.* The note climbed back to C. *Heave.* It stabilized, but the pressure outside was intensifying. The fog was swirling now, forming shapes—faces, buildings, cars from a century ago.

It was a cluster-memorie. A traumatic event trying to replay itself over the town.

She saw the shapes: horses galloping, the smell of smoke, the distinct sound of shattering glass. It was the Great Fire of '12, trying to happen again. If it touched the shore, the people of Oakhaven would wake up believing their houses were burning. Panic would ensue. The collective trauma would manifest physically, driven by the psychic energy of the ocean.

"Not tonight," Elara gritted out. She spun the wheel faster. The light above her flared, turning from a soft amber to a blinding, clarifying white.

The beam lanced out, cutting through the grey. It hit the swirling shapes of horses and smoke. There was a shriek—not of sound, but of psychic static—like the feedback of a microphone held too close to a speaker. The fog writhed.

Elara felt a tug at her own mind. The cluster-memorie was desperate. It reached for her, offering her a glimpse of her own past. *Take it,* the voice seemed to say. *You can be young again. You can see him.*

Her breath hitched. *Him.* David. He had been lost to the Drift twenty years ago, swept away while fishing too close to the edge of the safe zone. The temptation to drop the shield, to let the fog in just for a second, was a physical weight dragging her arms down.

"David," she whispered, tears pricking her eyes.

The fog swirled faster, forming his face. It was perfect—the crinkle around his eyes, the way his hair curled at the nape of his neck. He was smiling, holding out his hand.

*Elara. Come with me.*

Her grip on the crank loosened. The amber light flickered. The white beam wavered. The smell of smoke grew stronger. In the town below, a dog barked, sensing the shift in the air.

Elara stared at the image of her husband. It was everything she wanted. But then, she looked closer. The eyes were flat. They weren't looking at *her*; they were looking through her. It wasn't David. It was a recording. A loop. It was the ocean using her longing against her, trying to find a crack in the armor.

Anger flared in her chest, hot and sharp.

"No," she growled. "He is gone. This is a lie."

She slammed the lever forward, engaging the overdrive. The mechanism groaned, metal screaming against metal. The light exploded outward, a wall of pure, sonic truth.

The image of David screamed and dissolved. The horses retracted and vanished. The smoke curled back into the nothingness of the sea. The beam pushed the fog back half a mile, creating a bubble of crystal-clear air around the peninsula.

Elara slumped against the console, gasping for air. The resonance steadied at a perfect C, strong and true. The machinery cooled, ticking softly as it contracted.

Outside, the water was black and quiet. The fog was just a weather pattern now, harmless and wet.

She looked down at the town. The lights in the windows were still steady. The bakery was dark. The homes were silent. They were safe. They would wake up remembering who they were, what they loved, and where they belonged. They would remember their failures and their triumphs, but they would be their own memories, not the ocean's stolen scraps.

Elara wiped the sweat from her forehead. Her arms ached, a deep, bone-weary soreness that came with the job. She looked at the empty space where David’s face had been. It still hurt, the hollow space in her chest. But it was a *real* hurt. It was the price of love.

"Goodnight, David," she said to the dark. "Stay out there."

She turned from the glass and began the long descent down the spiral stairs. *Creak, step, creak, step.* The lighthouse held its breath behind her, waiting for the next tide, the next fog, the next ghost that tried to claim the living. But not tonight. Tonight, the past stayed in the past, and the future was left to sleep.
