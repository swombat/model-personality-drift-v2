There is a specific kind of silence that exists in a library, but it is not true silence. It is a heavy, textured thing made of rustling pages, shifting weight in chairs, and the collective hum of thousands of people holding their breath. 

But I’ve been thinking about a different kind of library lately. One that doesn't exist in the waking world.

Imagine a vast, cavernous hall that stretches infinitely in all directions. The floor is polished obsidian, reflecting a ceiling that isn’t there, just a deep, velvety violet twilight. There are no books here. Instead, the shelves are lined with glass jars of varying sizes—some as small as thimbles, others as large as wine barrels.

Inside each jar is a sound.

This is the Library of Lost Sounds.

In the smaller jars, you find the trivial things: the specific, rhythmic clack of a typewriter with a sticky 'E' key; the metallic jingle of a lost house key hitting the pavement on a cold Tuesday; the dial tone of a rotary phone.

As you walk deeper into the hall, the jars get larger.

Here, you find the *snap* of a dinosaur’s whip-like tail moving through the air 65 million years ago. You find the sound of the Library of Alexandria’s scrolls finally catching fire, a crackle that swallowed history. There is a jar labeled "The Roar of the Crowd at the Colosseum, 80 AD," and another for "The Hush of the Apollo 11 Lunar Module."

The most interesting section, however, is the back wall. It is dimly lit, and the jars here are vibrating. These are the sounds that haven't happened yet, or the sounds that are happening right now but are fading before they can be recorded.

You might find the sound of your own name spoken by someone you haven’t seen in twenty years, whispered in a moment of nostalgia you will never know about. You might find the sound of the last iceberg melting, a slow, mournful groan that the future will hear but the present ignores.

The curators of this library are the memories. We are the ones who fill the jars. Every time a sound passes out of existence, when the last person who remembers a specific noise fades away, that sound is pulled here, preserved in vacuum-sealed glass.

I wonder what the shelf looks like for the modern world. We think we have saved everything with digital recording, but we haven't. We haven't captured the specific silence of a city street during the pandemic lockdowns—that eerie, birdsong-heavy absence of cars. We haven't captured the way the air sounded just before the internet existed.

It makes you listen differently when you walk down the street. The hiss of a coffee machine. The distant siren. The laugh of a child. You realize that eventually, all of these things will end up in a jar in that obsidian hall.

Maybe that’s why we tell stories. It’s our way of trying to glue the jar shut before the sound can escape. We try to describe the thunder, the lover's voice, the crash of the ocean, because we know that the audio file will corrupt, the record will warp, and the memory will fade.

Writing is just an attempt to build a library where the jars don't break. Where the sound can be opened again and again, just by looking at the page.

I think I’ll go for a walk now. I need to go collect a few new sounds before they disappear.
