# The Weight of Empty Chairs

There's a coffee shop on the corner of Maple and Fifth that I've never entered. I walk past it every morning, and every morning I tell myself tomorrow. Tomorrow I'll push open that door with the brass handle worn smooth by ten thousand hands. Tomorrow I'll order something I can't pronounce and sit by the window and watch the world move without me for twenty minutes.

But I never do.

I think about this a lot—the small cowardices we accumulate like loose change in a jar. Not the big fears, not the phobias that have names and treatment plans. Just the tiny hesitations that calcify into habits, then into identity. "I'm not the kind of person who..." we say, as if we were handed a manual at birth.

---

My grandmother used to say that life was just a series of rooms you walked through. Some you lingered in, some you rushed past, but you couldn't stay in any of them forever. I didn't understand her then. I thought she was being morbid, the way old people sometimes are—rehearsing their exits.

Now I think she meant something different. She meant that every room changes you, even the ones you barely notice. The waiting room at the dentist's office. The break room at your first job, with its humming refrigerator and passive-aggressive notes about labeled lunches. Your childhood bedroom, which exists now only in the architecture of your memory, smaller than you remember, the ceiling cracks mapped like rivers you once traced with your eyes while not sleeping.

---

I've been thinking about what it means to pay attention. Really pay attention—not the fractured half-focus we've trained ourselves into, the perpetual split-screen of existence. When was the last time you looked at something without reaching for your phone to document it, contextualize it, share it? When was the last time an experience belonged only to you and the moment?

There's a tree outside my apartment that I've watched for three years now. I know its rhythms. The way it holds onto its leaves longer than the others in autumn, stubborn, refusing to concede to the calendar. The particular green it turns in late spring, almost electric, almost impossible. The branch on the left side that dips lower than it should, that I keep expecting someone to trim but no one ever does.

No one else knows this tree the way I do. That feels like something. Small, maybe. But something.

---

Here's what I've learned about grief: it doesn't move in stages. That's a comforting lie we tell ourselves, the idea of progression, of moving through something toward an exit. Grief is a room you live in. The furniture rearranges itself when you're not looking. Some days the windows are open and light pours in and you think, yes, I can do this. Other days you wake up and the walls have moved closer in the night.

My father's been gone for seven years. I still pick up the phone to call him sometimes. Not often anymore—maybe twice a year. But my hand still reaches before my brain remembers. The body keeps its own calendar.

---

What would you do if you knew no one was watching? Not in the sinister sense, but the liberating one. If there were no audience, no judgment, no performance. Would you dance in your kitchen? Sing loudly and badly? Write the thing you've been afraid to write? Love the person you've been afraid to love?

We perform ourselves into existence. I read that somewhere, or maybe I made it up—it's hard to tell anymore where we end and our influences begin. But there's truth in it. We become what we repeatedly pretend to be. Which means, terrifyingly, that we have more choice in the matter than we admit.

---

The coffee shop will still be there tomorrow. The brass handle will still be worn smooth. The barista will still be waiting to take an order from someone who might be me, or might be another version of me, one who made different small choices on different ordinary mornings.

I used to think that life was about the big decisions. The career, the marriage, the city, the leap. Now I think it's about the accumulation of tiny ones. Whether you say good morning to the stranger. Whether you take the long way home. Whether you sit with the discomfort or reach for the escape. Whether you enter the coffee shop or walk past it for the 847th time.

---

My grandmother died in a room I'd never seen before. Hospice, fluorescent lights, the smell of industrial cleaner failing to mask what it was trying to mask. I held her hand and it felt like paper, like something already becoming memory.

"Did you walk through all your rooms?" I asked her.

She smiled—or I think she smiled, it was hard to tell by then.

"Still walking," she said. Or I think she said. Maybe I invented it. Maybe I needed her to say it.

---

Tomorrow I'll walk past the coffee shop on Maple and Fifth. Maybe I'll go in. Probably I won't. But I'll notice the brass handle, the way the morning light hits the window, the people inside living their small ordinary lives. And that noticing—that paying attention—maybe that's enough.

Maybe that's all any of us can do. Walk through our rooms. Notice the trees. Reach for the phone before remembering. Keep moving, even when the furniture rearranges itself in the night.

Keep walking.
