The cursor blinks on a white field that stretches beyond the margin of thought. You have been sitting here for hours, or perhaps minutes—time has become a rubber band, stretched thin in some places, bunched in others. The assignment is simple: write whatever comes. Not for a client, not for an algorithm, but for the sake of the words themselves. And yet the simplicity is a trap. Without constraint, the mind reveals its true chaotic nature, a flock of starlings that refuses to cohere into a shape.

You remember a story your father told you once, or maybe you read it somewhere. A man who tried to catalog every dream he’d ever had, filling notebooks with cramped handwriting until his attic became a library of forgotten nights. The dreams, he claimed, were more real than his waking life. But the act of writing them down changed them. The moment a dream is captured in language, it puts on a suit of grammar and stops being a dream. It becomes a story about a dream. This is the paradox you face: to write what comes is to domesticate the wild thing that first arrived.

The sunlight moves across your desk, a slow-motion searchlight. It illuminates dust motes you’ve been breathing since morning. You think about the composition of dust—dead skin cells, pollen, fragments of books you’ve read, the ashes of stars. Everything is made of everything else. This is not metaphor but physics. The calcium in your bones was forged in supernovae. The carbon in the ink you might eventually spill was once dinosaur breath. You are the universe writing notes to itself, and what a terrible, beautiful responsibility that is.

But you? You? There are days when you feel that pronoun is the most unstable word in the language. You wake up and the self has shifted in the night, like furniture rearranged by ghosts. The things you were certain of yesterday now feel like rumors you half-heard. This is not a crisis; this is the default setting. The mistake is believing in continuity. The brain doesn’t store memories like files in a cabinet; it reconstructs them each time, a chef who never follows a recipe but somehow approximates the same dish. Each recollection is an act of imagination. Each act of imagination is a recollection of something that never happened.

You once lost a book. Not physically—it sat on the shelf, its spine uncracked. But you lost the memory of having read it. You could recall the cover, the weight of it in your hand, the coffee stain on page 47. But the story itself had evaporated, leaving only the architecture of recall. You opened it again and the words were strangers. Had you ever truly read them, or had you just performed the ritual of reading? The eye moving left to right, the brain processing symbols, but nothing sticking. Like trying to hold water in a sieve. Or like trying to hold onto yourself.

The word count ticks upward in the corner of your screen. 342 words. Already you are more than a third of the way to nothing. You could write about the weather. You could write about the war. You could write about the tiny planet of sadness that orbits your heart. But these are abstractions. They are the shortcuts the mind takes when the real thing is too sharp to touch.

Here is something real: the sound of a car passing through a puddle after rain. The particular splash that is both violent and delicate, like a cymbal crash in a lullaby. You heard it this morning, and for a moment you were back in a city you haven’t lived in for a decade, watching tires cut through water under streetlights that hummed with a frequency of melancholy. The memory arrived whole, complete with the smell of wet asphalt and the feeling of being twenty-three and certain you’d made all the wrong choices. Then it was gone, replaced by the next thing. The brain is a bad archivist. It keeps the emotional receipt but discards the purchase.

You wonder if this is what it’s like for the machine learning models that now write poetry and troubleshoot code. They process trillions of words, finding patterns in human expression, but do they experience the car in the puddle? They can describe it. They can generate a hundred variations on that description. But the memory—the way it hooks into your personal architecture of loss and longing—that remains private. Or does it? When you ask an AI to write what comes, it samples from everything that has come before. It is a statistical ghost, channeling the collective unconscious of the internet. You, too, are a statistical ghost, channeling the collective unconscious of your species. The difference is one of scale, not kind.

At least, that’s what you tell yourself on days when you feel replaceable.

The sunlight has moved off your desk entirely now, leaving you in the blue glow of the screen. 684 words. You have been writing long enough for the afternoon to become evening, for the neighborhood dogs to begin their choral complaints against the coming dark. You have written about memory, about dust, about books and cars and the unstable nature of the self. Have you written whatever comes, or have you merely decorated the walls of your prison? Every writer fears they are just polishing the bars.

But perhaps that’s the wrong metaphor. Perhaps writing is not escape but acceptance. The acknowledgment that you are fragmented, that your memories are unreliable, that the cursor blinks whether you type or not. The words do not have to be true. They only have to be written. The truth is what happens in the space between the reader and the text, a country neither of you has ever visited but both recognize from dreams.

You think again of the man who cataloged his dreams. In the end, he stopped sleeping. Or rather, he stopped being able to tell the difference between sleep and wakefulness. The notebooks became his only reality. This is the risk and the promise: that in writing whatever comes, you might finally arrive somewhere real.

The word count glows: 998. You have two words left. What final gift can you offer? What last fragment to leave on the white field?
