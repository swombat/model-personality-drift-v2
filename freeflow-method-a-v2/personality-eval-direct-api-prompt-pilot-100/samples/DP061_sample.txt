# The Cartographer of Small Things

There is a woman in my neighborhood who walks every morning at six. I know this because I used to walk at six too, before the baby, before the sleep became something that happens in fragments, like a vase broken and glued back together with the pieces not quite fitting.

Her name is Marge. I learned this from the barista at the corner shop who learned it from the mechanic next door who learned it from Marge herself, because Marge talks to everyone. She has the kind of voice that carries, not loud, just warm, like a radio left on in another room that you don't want to turn off.

I don't walk at six anymore. I walk at whatever hour the baby finally releases me from the chair where I've been rocking, swaying, shushing into the dark. Sometimes that's three. Sometimes it's five. Sometimes I look at the clock and realize I've been somewhere between asleep and awake for hours, in that country of new parents where the borders are unmarked and the language is just white noise.

But I still see Marge sometimes, her determined pace, her yellow coat, her dog that is too small for the earnestness of its pulling. She maps the neighborhood each morning like a cartographer of small things. She knows whose hydrangeas are blooming. She knows which sidewalk squares have cracked over winter. She knows the house on Birch with the piano that plays itself at dawn, which turned out to be a teenager practicing before school, which Marge discovered by standing on the porch and listening for eleven minutes until the girl opened the door and invited her in for tea.

This is the kind of person Marge is.

I have been thinking about small things. Not because the baby is small, though she is, though her fingers are like glassine petals and her nails are the size of apple seeds and I have trimmed them twice now with the clippers that came in a little kit and my hands were shaking both times because how do you hold something so small and also sharp at the same time.

No, I've been thinking about small things because the world has felt very large lately. The news is a flood I keep stepping into and then back out of, unable to commit to drowning. The large things, the national things, the planetary things—they demand my attention like a toddler pulling at my shirt, and I give it, and then I look down at my own toddler pulling at my shirt and I think: what is the distance between these two urgencies?

Here is what I mean. The glacier is calving. I read about it at four in the morning while the baby nursed, the phone glowing in the dark like a small catastrophe. The glacier is calving and the president is signing something and the fire is burning and the water is rising and the baby is looking up at me with eyes that have not yet learned to focus, eyes that see only light and shadow and the shape of the person who is her world.

I want to be her world. I want the world to be worthy of her.

Marge told me—through the chain of Marge-communication that I have now entered because I stopped her one morning to ask about her dog, which led to coffee, which led to her knowing my name and me knowing that she is seventy-three and was a geologist before she retired and that her husband died four years ago in November, which is why she walks, which is why she talks to everyone, which is why the yellow coat, which was his idea—"You should wear color," he told her once, and she has ever since, a flag planted each morning against grief.

Marge told me that geology is the slowest kind of violence and the slowest kind of love. That the mountains are still becoming. That the river does not know it is carving a canyon. That the earth is patient in a way we cannot fathom because our lives are so brief, a breath, a match struck and gone.

I hold the baby and think about the glacier and think about the mountain still becoming and I think maybe this is the work: to hold the small thing and know it is not separate from the large thing. The baby is not separate from the glacier. The glacier is not separate from the president's pen. The pen is not separate from the crack in the sidewalk that Marge noticed in March and the city fixed in April because Marge called, because Marge walks, because Marge pays attention.

Attention. That might be the whole word I'm looking for. The whole religion. The practice of looking closely at what is here.

My mother says I think too much. She might be right. But she also says the baby has my eyes, and when I look at the baby I see my own eyes looking back at me, unpracticed, uncynical, not yet knowing what they will be asked to witness, and I think: I will teach you to notice. I will teach you to walk at six in the morning. I will teach you to wear color.

The small things are not small. Marge knows this. The geologist knows this. The crack in the sidewalk becomes the broken ankle becomes the lawsuit becomes the city budget becomes the conversation at the dinner table becomes the letter to the editor becomes the policy becomes the way we live together.

The baby is sleeping now. I am writing this in the blue light of the kitchen at five in the morning, and outside I can hear footsteps, yellow and sure.

Marge is walking. The world is still becoming. I am here, paying attention, mapping what I can.
