On certain afternoons, I make tea as if it were a secret handshake with time. The kettle begins its small thunder, and the room holds its breath. I measure leaves like quiet punctuation, commas of green and brown that suggest a pause before meaning. When the water reaches that thin silver shimmer, I pour, and the steam lifts like a curtain, and the present enters, shy and clear eyed.

Outside, a delivery truck sighs at the curb. Someone laughs on a balcony. A sparrow lands, considers the railing, decides to stay. The world keeps rolling its marbles of noise, but the cup gathers a different gravity. Heat occupies my hands, convincing them of now. I wait for the swirl to settle. Waiting is unfashionable. It belongs to lines, to elevators, to the space after a text is sent. It is also where noticing grows legs.

I think about everything that asks to be immediate. Headlines, replies, reactions, the blue nudge of another tiny summons. The surface of attention becomes a busy harbor. Ships arrive already honking. In that harbor, a steeping minute feels like a small harbor within a harbor, where water thinks of being a mirror. I watch a bubble rise and go to its soft ending.

When the tea is ready, it does not announce a victory. It hums. I lift the cup. The first sip redraws the borders of the day, pulling them a little closer, not to wall anything out, but to keep one corner lit.
