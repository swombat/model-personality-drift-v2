The cursor blinks. That’s always where it starts—not with an idea, but with the blinking cursor, patient as a heartbeat, waiting for the blood to pump through. One thousand words. It feels like a budget, like someone handed me a roll of bills and said, spend it however you want, but when it’s gone, it’s gone. I keep thinking about that. Most of the time we write with an endless credit line, rambling on into the void, but here the void has a specific circumference.

This morning I woke up to the sound of a mockingbird. Not the pretty, looping call they make in April, but the harsh autumn scold, the sound of a creature annoyed that the berries are fermenting too early on the branch. There was light coming through the blinds in stripes, making the bedroom look like a cage for something valuable. I lay there and tried to remember a dream that had already evaporated, leaving only the emotional residue: the feeling of having forgotten something important in a house I no longer owned.

Memory is strange. It compresses. The decade I spent in my twenties feels now like a single long afternoon, humid and poorly lit, whereas an individual Tuesday from 2019 can expand to fill an entire room if I let it. I remember the Tuesday specifically because I was drying dishes with a towel that had a hole in it, and through that hole I could see the backyard, and the dog was digging a hole she would never finish. The towel was blue. Why do I remember the color of the towel but not the name of the man who taught me to drive? Priorities, I suppose. The brain curates a museum of its own choosing, and we are merely the janitors, wandering the halls after hours.

I read once that dust is mostly skin. We are constantly shedding ourselves into the environment, leaving microscopic evidence of our existence on every surface. The sunbeam in the kitchen this afternoon was full of this evidence—golden, swirling, a galaxy of dead cells and pollen and fibers from clothes we wore five years ago. I stood in it and thought: I am breathing myself in. There is a poetry to that, though maybe it’s only poetic because the word “dust” sounds soft. If we called it “human debris” the same sight would feel like a horror film.

There is a particular shade of blue that only exists in October, when the sky remembers how high it is. It’s deeper than September’s thin watercolor, more assertive than November’s gunmetal. I saw it today for exactly eleven minutes while I was driving to the pharmacy. The trees along the highway were starting to turn, but only on their outer edges, as if they were testing the temperature of the water with their toes. I wanted to pull over and take a photograph, but I know from experience that cameras can’t hold that blue. It slips through the lens like sand. Some things are only meant to be borrowed by the eye.

I think about the words left. The budget dwindles. It makes me anxious, which is absurd—words aren’t oxygen, though sometimes they feel like they are. When I was a child I would write stories in spiral notebooks, filling them with adventures that never ended because I never wanted to say goodbye to the characters. I still don’t. Endings feel artificial to me, like closing a door on a room where people are still talking. But everything ends. The mockingbird will stop scolding. The berries will fall. The sunbeam will shift off the kitchen table and the dust will settle into the cracks between the floorboards, patient, waiting to be disturbed again.

My grandmother used to save the last bite of every meal. She’d push it to the side of the plate and say it was for the angels, though we all knew she would wrap it in foil and eat it at midnight while reading library books she never returned. She believed in small hoardings, small savings. A thousand words would have felt extravagant to her, or perhaps terrifying. She preferred the economy of the unsaid. She could convey an entire tragedy by the way she folded a dish towel.

I wonder sometimes if the world is getting louder or if I am simply getting worse at filtering. There is a low hum everywhere now, an electrical thrum that lives behind the walls and in our pockets. Even in silence, we are not silent. I miss the kind of quiet that used to exist in houses before the internet, when the only sound at 2 AM was the refrigerator cycling on and the house settling its bones. That quiet had weight. You could feel it pressing against your eardrums, teaching you how to listen for your own pulse.

The cursor blinks. I check the count. We are somewhere in the middle now, past the point of no return but not yet within sight of the finish line. This is the dangerous territory where stories either find their spine or collapse into randomness. I want to tell you something true. I want to say that I am afraid, most of the time, of small things: phone calls from unknown numbers, the expiration date on yogurt, the way my knee makes a sound when I stand up that it didn’t used to make. I am afraid that I have already met the person who will matter most to me and failed to notice because I was looking at my phone. I am afraid that the particular angle of light I love most only happens at 4:47 PM in late October and I will be indoors when it arrives.

But I am also hopeful in a way that feels embarrassing to admit. I believe that if I keep describing things accurately enough, I might eventually get one thing exactly right. One sentence that feels like a door swinging open. One image that unlocks something in a stranger. That’s the gamble, isn’t it? We throw language at the void and hope it sticks. We write whatever comes to us because the alternative is to let the void win without a fight.

The mockingbird has stopped. The sky is darkening into a color I don’t have a name for—not navy, not violet, but something in between that only lasts for seven minutes. The kitchen light is on now, and the dust motes have disappeared into the artificial glow, waiting for morning. I am almost out of words. I can feel the limit approaching like a train in the distance, not threatening, just inevitable. I want to end with something lasting, but nothing lasts. So I will end with this: the towel had a hole in it, and through that hole I saw the dog digging, and the grass was very green, and the moment was ordinary and infinite, and I am still there, in some compressed version of myself, watching the earth turn over.
