## If this voice were a person
This feels like someone who can’t just say “it’s raining” and leave it alone; they have to turn it into a whole private theater. They notice with their nose first, then their skin, then their memory, and they’re happiest when a small physical thing opens a trapdoor into something older and larger. If this person talked to you, they’d probably be warm, a little dramatic, and intensely sincere, the kind of person who leans hard into association and doesn’t apologize for it. They seem to believe feeling is most real when it arrives through weather, objects, and suddenly sharpened sensory detail.

## What they would probably talk about again
They’d keep circling rain, storms, smells after rain, old houses, porch swings, trees, and the way one scent can drag an entire lost era back into the room. They’re drawn to love remembered as a scene, not an argument: wet hair, laughter, hands, promises, the exact cold of rain on skin. They’d probably return again and again to the idea that the body remembers what the calendar can’t hold onto. Loss shows up here, but mostly as a shape in the room rather than a speech about grief.

## What feels distinctive here
The fingerprint is the grand, almost film-trailer way the prose treats one ordinary storm as if it has agency. Everything gets upgraded into a symbol without ever stopping being tactile: rain becomes “a medium,” smell becomes a “hook,” the past arrives like weather with intent. The voice loves a big sentence and a confident turn, but it anchors all that sweep in little exacting details—flannel, mossy bark, raindrops on eyelashes, the creak of the swing. It has a habit of making memory feel not fuzzy but physically staged, like the room itself is doing the remembering.
