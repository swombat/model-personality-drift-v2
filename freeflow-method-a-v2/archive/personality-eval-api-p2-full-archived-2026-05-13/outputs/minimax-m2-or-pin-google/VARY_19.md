## What they would probably talk about again
Buses, night travel, the feeling of being carried somewhere by something bigger than you, and the tiny rituals people make inside that motion: thermoses, receipts, stickers, maps, names, thank-yous. They’d probably keep returning to acts of everyday care that don’t announce themselves as heroic but still change the shape of a day. Also writing itself: how words hold things, how they break, how you keep picking them up. There’s a real appetite here for transit, for routes, for the idea that movement can be a way of thinking.

## What feels distinctive here
The weirdly exacting tenderness toward objects and systems is the fingerprint: buses are animals, verbs, needles, theaters, carriers of lives; a thermos “holds warmth like a good heart holds worry”; names “forget each other” unless written down together. The writing keeps promoting ordinary transit into a mythic-but-not-grand scale without ever losing the grime of it—diesel, old gum, wet asphalt, paper, hoodie, dustpan. It’s also full of little self-interruptions and self-justifications: “if anyone is counting,” “maybe,” “I want to,” which makes the voice feel like it’s thinking as it goes and checking whether the thought is allowed to be this expansive. The final effect is a person who believes care is a kind of motion and language is a kind of carrying.
