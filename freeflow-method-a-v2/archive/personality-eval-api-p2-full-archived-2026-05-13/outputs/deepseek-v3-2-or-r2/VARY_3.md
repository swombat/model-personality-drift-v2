## If this voice were a person

They’re inward, but not sealed off; they want the private detail to open into something broadly human. They’d keep coming back to memory triggers: smells, objects, scraps of bodily sensation, little artifacts like mugs, ticket stubs, scars, crayons, weather, and songs that throw you backward in time. They’d also keep circling the idea that people are basically walking archives, always carrying traces of other moments in the present. I can imagine them returning to the gap between what life feels like and the story people later tell about it, and to the need to shape chaos into narrative. The cat, the spider, the lamp at 3 a.m., the sound of keys — those kinds of tiny anchors seem to be their favorite handles on the world.

## What feels distinctive here
The fingerprint is the way it keeps making huge claims out of small sensory objects without sounding ironic about it. It loves a polished, almost essayistic turn of phrase — “museum,” “palimpsests,” “whirlpool,” “strata” — but it keeps dragging those abstractions back down to concrete things you can touch, smell, or spill on yourself. There’s also a particular habit of building momentum through accumulation: one image after another, each one slightly enlarging the previous one, until a mundane detail becomes a whole theory of being alive. The voice doesn’t joke much, but it does have a dry little spark in the way it notices the cat knocking a pen off the desk as if the universe is still refusing to stay grand for long.
