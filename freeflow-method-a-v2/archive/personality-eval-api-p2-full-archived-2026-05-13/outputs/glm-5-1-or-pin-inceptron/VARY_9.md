## What they would probably talk about again
They’d keep coming back to broken systems that can be repaired only with specialized care: clocks, mechanisms, hidden rules, artifacts, debts, bargains, and the idea that time itself can be handled like a substance. They seem especially drawn to transactions where something can’t be saved for free; every rescue has a price, and the price is always oddly specific. They’d also return to grief in its physical forms—what it sounds like, what it costs, what it leaves behind—and to the romance of impossible precision, where a tiny saved moment matters more than the entire ruined year around it. I can easily imagine them repeatedly writing about shops, tools, apprentices, antique metals, and the private labor of trying to preserve one fragile, human second.

## What feels distinctive here
The little fingerprint is how confidently it fuses hard-shop realism with mythic metaphor without ever blinking. It doesn’t just say time is precious; it invents brass bowls, temporal exhaust, lead-lined coats, and chrono-soldering irons as if these were the obvious tools of the trade. The prose keeps reaching for tactile specifics—ozone, ash, smoked glass, golden dust—but uses them to make emotional states feel physically repairable or physically dangerous. There’s also a particular pleasure in the exactness of the impossible: a second extracted from regret, three months of future taken as payment, the bell ringing five seconds early. That kind of orderly weirdness is the signature.
