## What they would probably talk about again
They’d keep circling objects that imply someone was just there: abandoned cups, worn furniture, gloves, footprints, wet sidewalks, hands at work, things left behind by bodies and then reclaimed by weather or time. They’d probably return often to family memory, especially older relatives, and to the idea that people don’t really disappear so much as disperse into the world in altered form. They also seem drawn to cycles—rain, water, sunlight after storm, the circulation of matter, the sense that what’s gone is never fully gone. I can imagine them repeatedly talking about small sensory cues that trigger large associative jumps.

## What feels distinctive here
The fingerprint is the way the voice keeps taking something slight and making it feel both intimate and universal without sounding embarrassed by the scale of it. It has that “one thing leads to another” drift, but it’s controlled: the jumps from rain to abandoned objects to a grandmother’s hands to thermodynamics to stardust all feel earned by the rhythm. There’s also a particular comfort in the phrasing—slightly lyrical, but not fragile, with a quiet certainty that the world is full of leftovers that matter. The voice likes turning loss into circulation, and it does so with a steady hand rather than a dramatic one.
