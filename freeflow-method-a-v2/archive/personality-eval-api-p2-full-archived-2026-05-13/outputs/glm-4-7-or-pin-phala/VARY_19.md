## If this voice were a person
This feels like someone who has learned to make a room out of objects: tools laid in neat lines, old brass rubbed bright by hand, a habit of talking about broken things as if they have tempers and memories. He’s dry, a little prickly, and clearly allergic to sentimentality in the moment, but he keeps slipping into it anyway—then pretending he didn’t. The voice likes competence, texture, and the exact right word for a spring or hinge, but underneath that there’s a person who is more moved than he wants to admit by grief showing up in ordinary packaging. He sounds old enough to be impatient with nonsense, young enough in spots to still be surprised by tenderness, and lonely in the way of someone who has spent too long listening to mechanical things instead of people.

## What they would probably talk about again
Broken clocks, bad repairs, and the weird emotional life of objects. He’d keep circling back to time as a physical nuisance and a philosophical bully: things run down, springs fail, people try to pretend they can keep control, they can’t. He’d also probably keep telling stories about customers who arrive carrying damaged items like they’re carrying damaged lives—grandfathers, radios, watches, music boxes, any small machine that can stand in for a larger mess. And he’d make a point of the shop itself: the smell of oil and brass, the dust, the invoices, the inventory, the aggravation of drawers full of screws.

## What feels distinctive here
The fingerprint is the way the voice keeps turning repair into near-liturgy without ever fully dressing it up as such. It has a gruff, practical surface—“five hundred,” “pass me the small screwdriver”—but it keeps reaching for sly little lines that make the objects feel almost alive: bells that cough, clocks that hold grudges, a mechanism treated like surgery. There’s also a nice habit of undercutting itself: a big time/riverrun speech, then an immediate grim little correction that strips the poetry back down. That push-pull between bluntness and lyric detail is what gives it its shape.
