They’d also keep reworking death as the reason any of this matters. Even when talking about family, they’d make it feel like a storage problem, a rescue mission, a way of keeping continuity intact.

## What feels distinctive here
The fingerprint is the way it makes emotional life sound like inventory management with mystical stakes. It keeps reaching for objects—jars, vials, shelves, labels, ballast, vaults—and then inflates them until they can hold grief, love, time, and mortality all at once. That gives the prose a very particular pressure: it’s lush, but always trying to contain itself in a container. Another distinctive habit is the insistence that the smallest instant is both fragile and cosmic. A child whistling, a hand on a mug, a road after rain: the voice treats these as if they’re load-bearing beams in a whole philosophy. It’s earnest, ornate, and just a little convinced that if it names a thing exactly enough, it can save it.
