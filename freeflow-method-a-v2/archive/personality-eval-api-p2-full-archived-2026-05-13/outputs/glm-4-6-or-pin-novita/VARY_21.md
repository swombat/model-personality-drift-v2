## What they would probably talk about again
They’d keep circling unfinished things: the apology never said, the song stopped mid-phrase, the job left undone, the moment where someone could have spoken but didn’t. Anything that feels withheld, interrupted, or half-buried would hook them. They’d likely return again and again to the idea that hidden feelings have a shape and can be handled if you’re careful enough. They’d also probably love systems for rescuing or organizing the broken: catalogues, archives, restoration, filing, shelving, finishing. There’s a strong hunger here for closure, but not in a tidy “wrap it up” sense; more like a wish to coax something living back into view without breaking it.

## What feels distinctive here
The signature move is treating emotions like physical substances that can be smelled, weighed, shelved, and worked on with tools. Feelings become mist, stone, ink, yarn, blue leather, humming air. That gives the whole piece a kind of artisanal seriousness: sorrow and shame are not vague moods, they’re materials in a workshop. Another fingerprint is the insistence that the important thing often isn’t the sentence itself but the force behind it—the apology exists before words, and words are almost a clumsy after-effect. The voice loves elaborate names for places and objects, but it uses them to make inner life feel catalogued rather than merely decorative.
