## If this voice were a person
This feels like someone who can’t help turning life into a lantern-lit anecdote, even when the thing itself is messy, practical, or sad. They’re observant to the point of ornamental, always catching the exact color of the sky, the smell of old wood, the weird dignity of a broken appliance, then pinning that detail to a bigger feeling before you’ve had time to blink. They seem a little overeducated, a little under-rested, and faintly amused by their own dramatics—aware they’re being florid, but choosing it anyway because plain speech doesn’t feel strong enough for what they’re carrying. Under the sparkle there’s a person who is attachment-heavy, death-aware, and forever trying to rescue meaning from objects, buildings, and family leftovers before the bulldozer arrives.

## What they would probably talk about again
They’d come back to transit and in-between places: rooftops, highways, abandoned storefronts, rain-slick parking lots, attics, ferry docks, the exact hour when dusk turns electrical. They’d also keep talking about old family holdings that have become haunted by memory—a grandmother’s shop, a trunk, a daybook, a fridge badge, a card in a Rolodex—because they seem convinced that objects store a version of the people who touched them. Expect repeated returns to loss, inheritance, and the weird shame of being late to preserve something. They’d probably also narrate small bodily panics with comic timing: a tire blowing, numb hands, client deadlines, awkward therapy advice, the feeling of being both ridiculous and briefly heroic.

## What feels distinctive here
The fingerprint is how eagerly the voice fuses the lyrical and the slapstick without ever fully switching registers. A bald little practical errand can become a mythic pilgrimage, then collapse into an exhausted joke about gumption, then bloom back into weather and birds and dust. It loves making up lush comparisons, but the comparisons are oddly busy and handmade rather than polished—“stale cheerios of light,” “water-boarded by an overhead cloud,” “half-thief, half-child.” That mix of grand self-story and self-mockery gives it a very particular energy: someone writing as if they’re trying to out-collect time with language, while knowing that’s probably impossible.
