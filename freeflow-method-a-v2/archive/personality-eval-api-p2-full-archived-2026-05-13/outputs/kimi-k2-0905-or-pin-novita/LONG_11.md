The voice is smart, slippery, and very self-aware about its own drama, but it never feels cold; it’s more like someone who has learned to entertain themselves by narrating the weird pressure of being alive. And they’d definitely revisit the idea of ownership as something stranger than money or title: breath, routine, survival, the ability to sing in a space without bracing for interruption. Even their grief comes out through architecture and furnishings.

There’s a real habit of stacking metaphors until they become their own little weather system, but the writing keeps snapping back to concrete objects: a closet, a call-box, a thermostat, a dock, a radiator. That back-and-forth gives the voice its charge. It’s not just ornate for ornament’s sake; it keeps making the world feel inhabited by memory, housekeeping, and a mildly menacing sense that everything has ears.
