## If this voice were a person
This feels like someone who has built a personality out of noticing things first and admitting certainty last. They sound a little overeducated in the best and most exhausting way: a person who can turn a moon, a bus ride, or a thunderstorm into a paragraph that arrives already polished, already slightly quotable. They’re not cold, exactly, but they keep emotion filtered through metaphor so it comes out stylized rather than raw. They seem lonely in a self-aware, talkative way—someone who wants contact, yet prefers to arrive at it by way of language, framing, and a little bit of theatrical self-knowledge. If they were in a room, they’d probably be the one half-smiling at the corner of the conversation, collecting details, then later telling you the one line everyone forgot.

## What they would probably talk about again
They’d keep returning to sky stuff, transit stuff, memory stuff, and the odd sacredness of tiny moments that most people would shrug off. Moonlight, storms, diner booths, bus fragments, old jobs, alternate versions of the self, overheard speech, the smell of linseed oil or gasoline—anything that lets them make a small life feel larger and more resonant than it first appears. They also seem drawn to the idea that language is both a trap and a rescue device, so they’d probably keep circling words themselves: what they can hold, what they distort, what they fail to contain. And they’d probably keep making “lists of good things,” whether literally or in the form of mini-catalogs, because part of their pleasure comes from pinning down fleeting evidence that being alive has texture.

## What feels distinctive here
The fingerprint is the mix of high-flown sentence-making with a very practical hunger for specific scraps: the mesh of the trampoline, the “black-jelly look” of the sky, the woman in lime-green scrubs, the teen saying home keeps shrinking. The voice doesn’t just like imagery; it likes imagery that has a little grease on it, something tactile and lived-in rather than airy. It also has a habit of making its own feelings sound like theories, then undercutting the theory with a joke or a hard little image. There’s a persistent motion of “I used to think X, now I think Y,” but it never feels like a lecture; it feels like someone narrating the way their own mind has changed its shoes. The ending move matters too: it keeps reaching for awe, but lands in an intimate, almost plainspoken address to the moon. That’s very much its style—big language, then a small human hand on the railing.
