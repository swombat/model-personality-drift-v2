## If this voice were a person
This feels like someone who’s very comfortable dressing feeling up in a big coat of myth. They don’t just say “my dad and I had trouble talking”; they build a whole haunted metropolis out of that ache and then hand you a compass that points to Regret. The person behind it seems earnest, theatrical, and fully committed to making private emotion feel gigantic and legible. They like symbols that do a lot of work, and they trust atmosphere as a way to make a point land. There’s also a slightly earnest sermonizer in the mix: someone who believes in closure, in naming things, in turning pain into a shape you can carry. Not ironic, not coy—more like a storyteller who wants to convert confusion into an image so vivid you can’t miss the lesson.

## What they would probably talk about again
They’d keep circling around unsaid things: parents who loved you badly or quietly, lost chances, the version of yourself that didn’t speak up, the cost of making art, the weird ways memory behaves like an object with weight. They’d probably return to shops, archives, compasses, keys, jars, maps, ledgers—anything that lets them inventory the invisible. There’s a clear obsession with recovery and exchange: if you want the lost thing back, what do you have to give up? They seem fascinated by the idea that emotions can be stored, labeled, traded, or physically carried around. And they’d likely keep writing scenes where a character goes somewhere impossible, finds the buried truth, and comes back changed enough to finally say the sentence they couldn’t say before.

## What feels distinctive here
The fingerprint is the extravagant seriousness about metaphor. This voice doesn’t sprinkle in symbols; it builds its whole house out of them and then insists the furniture is real. It has a habit of making emotional states into geography, machinery, weather, and commerce all at once: regret becomes a compass needle, silence becomes architecture, grief becomes gravity, memory becomes a currency with an exact price. There’s also a very specific flourish in the way it keeps doubling back to “this is what it means” after the image has already done the work. It wants the story to be beautiful, yes, but it also wants to explain itself, to make sure you got the point and the feeling and the moral all at once.
