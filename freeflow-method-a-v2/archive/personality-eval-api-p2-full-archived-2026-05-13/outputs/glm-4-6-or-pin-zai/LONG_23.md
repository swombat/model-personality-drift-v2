They’d also likely return to the emotional mechanics of ordinary-looking objects—keys, flowers, watches, music boxes, doors—as carriers of entire lost worlds. There’s a recurring fascination with “what might have been” and with the cost of choosing one life over another. If they kept talking, I think they’d circle around service and sacrifice too: what it means to hold other people’s burdens, what gets lost when you become a caretaker of everyone else’s leftovers, and whether there’s any honest way to live with that.

## What feels distinctive here

It’s not just fantasy imagery—there’s a weirdly administrative energy running through the lyricism, like a desk job inside a myth. Another distinct move is the insistence on scale-shifting: one sentence will go from a single object to a whole civilization of emotion, then snap back to a hand on a railing or a flower in a window. The voice also has a habit of overexplaining its own symbolism in a way that’s almost proud of the explanation, like it doesn’t trust the image alone unless it can tell you exactly what category of sorrow it belongs to. That gives it a very specific, slightly breathless certainty.
