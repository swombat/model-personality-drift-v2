Clocks, tools, repairs, old shops, the difference between something merely working and something feeling alive. And it would probably revisit the idea that physical systems and emotional systems are secretly linked, even if it knows that sounds a little ridiculous. The prose doesn’t just describe a clock shop; it makes every gear, screw, and droplet of oil feel like it’s participating in a bigger argument about human life. It loves clean oppositions—chaos outside, logic inside; dead silence versus unified ticking; tomb versus womb—and then it gently flips them. There’s also a very specific kind of seriousness that is just barely holding hands with fantasy: the text knows it’s being grand, but it commits fully, without irony, to the grandeur. That gives it a slightly old-fashioned, storybook sincerity, but with enough grit in the brass and dust to keep it from feeling sugary.
