## What they would probably talk about again
Closed businesses, familiar places changing shape, names of old uses under new paint, the strange comfort of being recognized by a barista or cashier, and how quickly a place becomes a memory once the chairs are gone. They’d keep circling the idea that we leave traces without meaning to, and that the same corner can hold several lives at once. I’d expect them to return to childhood-sized moments of looking in windows, walking past places after a breakup or a death, and that quiet fact that people and rooms both get replaced. They seem drawn to the afterimage: what’s left when the thing itself is already moving on.

## What feels distinctive here

That combo—elegant abstraction anchored by very ordinary, touchable bits—gives it its shape. It also has a mild self-correction built in: “Not in a sad way, exactly,” as if the speaker is always gently negotiating with their own sentimentality rather than surrendering to it.
