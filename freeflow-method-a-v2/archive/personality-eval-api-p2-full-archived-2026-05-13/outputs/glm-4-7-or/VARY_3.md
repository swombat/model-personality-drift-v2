They seem dignified, a little old-fashioned, and quietly dramatic in the way they assign meaning to objects. Not showy, but very committed to the idea that the world is full of traces and that someone has to care about them.

## What they would probably talk about again
They’d keep coming back to lost things, broken things, and the strange emotional charge of objects that outlive the people attached to them. They like the story hiding inside a pocket watch, a seed, a letter, a key, a wedding dress, a child’s tooth. They’d probably return over and over to the tension between keeping and releasing, between cataloging and rescuing, between protocol and the urge to do something human. They also seem drawn to old buildings, sealed rooms, archives, ledgers, and any place where the past is physically stored instead of merely remembered. The modern city would show up too, but mostly as a noisy force pressing in from outside.

## What feels distinctive here

It keeps making the same move: begin with a cool, meticulous description, then suddenly let one object or detail crack the whole system open. The prose is full of weighty textures—velvet, brass, stone, warped glass, cold metal—and it keeps staging a clash between hush and noise, stillness and motion, keeping and letting go. Even when it gets sentimental, it does it through inventory and procedure rather than direct confession.
