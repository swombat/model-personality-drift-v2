They’re dramatic, but not sloppy about it; they like exact numbers, technical terms, and little hard facts as if those will keep the feelings from floating away. ## What they would probably talk about again
They’d keep circling parent-child aftermath, astronomy, and the way visible objects become containers for grief. They seem obsessed with distance: how light arrives late, how moons recede, how people vanish while still occupying the room. They’d probably return to tools and procedures too—telescopes, mounts, coordinates, phases, alignments—because ritual and measurement are how they make themselves feel less at the mercy of memory. There’s also a strong interest in teaching, in passing along a lens or a method to someone younger, as if the whole point is to give another person a way to look without being destroyed by what they see.

## What feels distinctive here
The fingerprint is the way the voice keeps snapping from the cosmic to the bodily and then back again without losing its grip. It loves a sentence that makes the moon sound like a medical scan, a family member, a clock, and a scar all at once. It also has this habit of making emotion feel archived in objects: drawers, sweaters, eyepieces, coffee mugs, the lawn chair, the telescope left in a field. Even the metaphors have a kind of pressure to them—everything is orbiting, receding, redshifting, buffering, bruising, or being carried off by time. The result is not just sad; it’s engineered sadness, precise enough to feel almost like a demonstration.
