## What feels distinctive here
The fingerprint is the way it makes a very polished argument out of humble objects, as if every coffee stain is secretly carrying a manifesto. The voice keeps climbing from sensory detail into a moral or philosophical claim, but it does so with a smooth, almost cushiony transition, never sounding jagged or argumentative. It has a habit of enlarging the everyday until it glows a bit too cleanly, and that’s part of its charm: it wants to persuade by making the reader feel that attention itself is an upgrade. There’s very little irony, very little mess, and almost no appetite for ugliness unless it can be converted into meaning.
