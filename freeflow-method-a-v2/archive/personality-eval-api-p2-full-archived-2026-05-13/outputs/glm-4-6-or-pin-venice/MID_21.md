They’d keep circling the idea that things hold people inside them after the people are gone, and that the real story is often not the official one but the rubbed-away one. They’d also come back to the fantasy of the stranger’s life: who owned it, what they felt, whether it was love or grief or secrecy. There’s also a soft, elegant overstatement to it: “morgue of moments,” “silent orchestra,” “antique shops for the moments we’ve lived.” That’s the style: lush but controlled, always reaching for one more image that makes the same feeling deeper and stranger.
