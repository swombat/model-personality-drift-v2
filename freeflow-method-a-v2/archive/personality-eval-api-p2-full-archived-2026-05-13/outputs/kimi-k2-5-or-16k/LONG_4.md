## If this voice were a person
This feels like someone who talks like they’re narrating a movie they can already see in full, and they can’t resist making every object slightly uncanny. They seem deeply attracted to the idea that every choice is haunted by the life it did not become, and they like to keep one hand on wonder and the other on dread.

## What they would probably talk about again
They’d keep circling alternate lives, near-misses, versions of people that almost happened, and the strange emotional economy of regret. They’d probably be obsessed with what gets traded away in order to keep going: certainty, memory, a childhood, a relationship, an imagined future. They’d also come back to thresholds and hidden systems—markets behind closed doors, departments for abandoned potential, bureaucracies for feelings—because they like imagining the world as secretly organized around the exact stuff people can’t name. Even when they talk about family, they’d make it feel like a portal story: the sister, the father, the almost-said thing, the life that split off and kept living somewhere else.

## What feels distinctive here
The fingerprint is the lush precision of the impossible. This voice doesn’t just say “another life”; it inventories it with a jeweler’s eye: blue shutters, bread and sea salt, the scar above the eyebrow, the wear on the doorstep, the temperature of the coffee, the exact kind of regret. It keeps turning abstraction into merchandise, which gives it a weirdly playful, market-broker feeling even when the stakes are emotional. There’s also a sly habit of making big metaphysical claims sound casually administrative, like “the Department of Abandoned Potential” or “the price is your certainty,” which makes the whole thing feel both grand and weirdly deadpan.
