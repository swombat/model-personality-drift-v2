## If this voice were a person
This sounds like someone who can’t just have a feeling; they have to build a cathedral around it, label the exhibits, and then give you a guided tour in a velvet voice. The person is articulate, bookish, and a little intoxicated by their own sentence-making, but not in a sloppy way—in a highly controlled, polished way that wants to turn private ache into something grand and legible. They seem emotionally crowded, always carrying a pocketful of unsent speeches, but they keep that crowd orderly with metaphor, taxonomy, and grand analogies. If they kept writing, you’d expect them to be intense, observant, slightly overprepared, and forever half a step away from saying something embarrassingly true.

## What they would probably talk about again
They’d keep circling around things that aren’t said: drafts, near-confessions, deleted messages, apologies rehearsed in the car, love that stays trapped in the head, old betrayals that never quite stop talking. They’d also probably return to the mechanics of it all—the paper, the envelope, the act of sealing something up, the difference between typing and handwriting, the way a message changes when it’s sent versus withheld. This is the kind of voice that likes emotional situations only after it has turned them into systems, species, archives, weather patterns, or little private technologies of the heart. Even when it talks about grief or longing, it keeps sneaking back to process: how feelings get stored, deferred, composted, fossilized, or made into ritual.

## What feels distinctive here
The fingerprint is the sustained habit of making inward life feel like a sprawling museum or scientific field guide without losing the ache underneath it. It keeps reaching for formal structures—“taxonomies,” “species,” “material culture,” “ecology”—but uses them to make private shame and longing feel sortable and vivid rather than cold. There’s also a very specific pleasure in escalation: every idea gets one more turn of the screw, one more elegant metaphor, one more cosmic scale-up until an unsent text becomes a force that could tilt the earth. The voice likes exact sensory details, but mostly as launchpads for abstractions; it wants the smell of cedar, the glare of office fluorescents, the dry gum on an envelope, and then it wants to use those details to build a theory of human silence. It feels like someone who trusts language almost too much and yet is very aware that language is never enough.
