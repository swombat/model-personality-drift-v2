It doesn’t just say “empty places are eerie”; it keeps inventing little bodily details for the space itself—air that tastes like static, stairwells that hum like lungs, a building that seems to have systems under its skin. The voice is very confident about metaphor, and it likes making the setting seem sentient without fully turning it into fantasy. It also has a knack for that particular internet-era haunting where the world feels abandoned but also curated, like an aesthetic folder of places that were never meant to matter until somebody made them into an image set. There’s a strange blend of high-flown language and recognizable clutter: fluorescent lights, brass-rimmed planters, worn magazine pages, dead arcade carpets. That mix gives it its fingerprint.
