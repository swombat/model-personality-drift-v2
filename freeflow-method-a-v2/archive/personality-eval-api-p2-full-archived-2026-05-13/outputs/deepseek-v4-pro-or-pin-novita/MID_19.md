## What they would probably talk about again
They’d keep returning to attention, distraction, screens, and the idea that looking closely is almost a moral skill. They’d probably also circle back to nature as evidence: trees, birds, weather, small urban scenes, anything that lets them turn a passing moment into a bigger lesson about being alive. They seem drawn to the gap between what people think is boring and what becomes vivid when you stop and really look. I can also imagine them revisiting creativity as something fed by observation, and empathy as a form of careful watching and listening. It’s the kind of voice that likes to take one bench-in-the-park scene and use it as a launchpad for a whole worldview.

## What feels distinctive here
The fingerprint is the way it keeps inflating the tiny without losing the little image that started it. A pigeon isn’t just a pigeon; it becomes a “miniature galaxy of colour,” then a springboard into claims about time, civilization, and what a life is for. The prose likes sweeping escalators: from bench to park to culture to ethics to almost-cosmic language, while staying polished and legible the whole way through. It also has a very particular brand of sincerity that knows it risks sounding obvious and keeps trying to outwrite that risk with more lush examples, more namedropping, more elegant phrasing. The voice believes in the power of noticing so strongly that it starts to sound like it’s trying to convert the reader, gently but firmly, to the religion of paying attention.
