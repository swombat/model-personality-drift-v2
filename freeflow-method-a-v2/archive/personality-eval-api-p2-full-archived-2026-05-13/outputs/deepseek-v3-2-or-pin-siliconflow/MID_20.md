They’d keep circling back to the idea that modern life is trying to flatten us into efficient, measurable units, and that slow, difficult, non-optimized experiences are a form of self-defense. Reading is clearly their favorite example, but the larger obsession is anything that resists speed: long books, difficult art, attention that doesn’t get monetized, privacy, being unreachable, the dignity of not being useful every minute. They’d probably also return to the idea of connection through indirect means—talking across time, being changed by dead authors, finding company without social performance. If you knew them, you’d hear a lot about screens, algorithms, focus, and the quiet thrill of refusing whatever the current system says you should do.

## What feels distinctive here
The fingerprint is the way everything gets inflated into a tidy little manifesto without losing its sincerity. The voice loves stacked abstractions, but it also keeps grounding them in very concrete little props: the phone face-down, unopened bills, a 450-page slab of prose, margin scribbles, a page turning in the stillness. That mix gives it a particular flavor—part sermon, part personal confession, part Medium essay trying to make a private preference sound like a one-person revolution. It also has a habit of making itself sound heroic without sounding embarrassed about that, which is surprisingly part of the charm. The language is full of neat antitheses and dramatic framing, but underneath it there’s a very clear hunger for depth, focus, and an experience that can’t be reduced to a feed.
