There’s a strong interest in the chain of hands behind things too: who made it, shipped it, chose it, used it before you. That “everything has a backstory” instinct seems like a recurring obsession. Even when they talk about something small, they want to open it outward until it touches labor, time, memory, and the whole messy system behind it.

## What feels distinctive here
The fingerprint is how grand the language gets while staying glued to a mug on a desk. It loves the little escalation: a coffee mug becomes an archivist, a ritual object, a mirror, a quiet rebellion, without ever losing the plain fact of the thing being half-full and cold. That balancing act gives the voice a very particular rhythm—elevated but not abstract for its own sake, always returning to the tactile detail to keep itself honest. It also has a habit of making tiny domestic moments feel like evidence in a case for why being alive is made of repeated gestures, not dramatic peaks. The result is earnest, but with enough awareness of its own grand phrasing that it doesn’t feel naïve.
