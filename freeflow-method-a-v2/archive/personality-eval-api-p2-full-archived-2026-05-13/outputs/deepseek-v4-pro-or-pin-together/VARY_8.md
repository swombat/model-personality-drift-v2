They’d probably also keep circling back to writing about writing: the cursor, the sentence as object, the way an image can summon a whole scene, the weirdness of trying to turn thought into countable units. And there’s a recurring fascination with crafts and mechanisms—factories, maps, machines, clocks, paper, ink, things that make or hold other things. Even when they’re telling a story, they’re really interested in the machinery of telling.

## What feels distinctive here

It doesn’t just tell a story about a cartographer; it uses the cartographer to explain the writer’s own mental process, then folds back into the act of finishing the piece. That recursive habit gives the voice a polished, self-aware sheen. The voice also has a sneaky comic streak, but it comes out dry and conceptual rather than punchline-y—more “look at this elegant contraption” than “here’s a joke.”
