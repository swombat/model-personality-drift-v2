## If this voice were a person
This feels like someone who can’t leave a plain thought alone for long—they keep picking it up, turning it over, and hanging a metaphor on it. They’re bookish, a little dazzled by their own mind, and very tuned to texture: blinking cursors, blue airmail paper, buttons, moss, fog, afternoon light. There’s a nervous energy under all that beauty, like they’re trying to talk themselves into finishing by making the idea larger and more poetic than the task deserves. They come off as observant and sincere, but also self-conscious in a way that makes them keep reaching for another image instead of saying the blunt thing directly.

## What they would probably talk about again
Writing itself, definitely: word counts, endings, first words, the pressure of having to make language do something exact. Also old objects that feel charged with hidden lives—encyclopedias, jars of buttons, letters, basement shelves, things that carry time in them. They seem likely to return to the idea that meaning leaks, gets cut, gets lost, or survives in scraps. And they’d probably keep circling the same question: how do you say something true when the tools you have are too small, too cheap, or too full of noise?

## What feels distinctive here
The fingerprint is how seriously it treats metaphor as a working method, not decoration. Almost every paragraph starts with a practical anxiety—count the words, finish the piece, find the ending—and then immediately expands into a lush little machine of comparisons that tries to solve the anxiety by widening the frame. The voice also has a habit of making invisible things physical: missed words become weather, language becomes tide, memory becomes archaeology. It’s poetic, but in a slightly overdetermined, restless way, like someone thinking at full speed and refusing to let one image sit still.
