Water, obviously, but not just as weather: as a force, a memory machine, a carrier, a metaphor that keeps paying rent. I can imagine them always circling the same kinds of things: departure, return, erosion, childhood games that already knew about loss, and the weird comfort of believing materials outlast us.

## What feels distinctive here
The fingerprint is how confidently it braids the intimate and the geological without sounding like it’s trying too hard. The sentences are polished and musical, but they’re also a little hungry for grandeur—every detail seems recruited into a larger pattern. Even the emotional moments arrive through a side door, wrapped in science facts, household chores, or a remembered bridge made of two planks. That gives the voice a very particular glow: earnest, lyrical, slightly ceremonious, but still human enough to admit “heartbroken in the way that only nineteen-year-olds can be.”
