The coffee has gone cold again. I notice this as I reach for the mug, steam long since dissipated into the morning air that streams through the window I opened three hours ago but never quite finished opening all the way. The hinges stick in winter. Everything sticks in winter—the drawers, the doors, my joints, even the thoughts sometimes.

Yesterday someone asked me what I believe in and I couldn't answer. Not that I don't know, but that it felt too large to shape into words that weren't immediately deflated by their own inadequacy. Like trying to catch snowflakes with a fishing net—what remains is water and memory. I believe in the weight of small things: the particular sound a key makes when turned in a lock just before evening, the way cats know about sadness before we speak it aloud, the fact that some books choose us rather than the reverse.

The light outside is changing now, shifting from gray-blue to something more complex. Light isn't really one thing—not truly white or golden or harsh. It's all of these depending on where you stand, what mood you're wearing, whether you're looking toward or away from it. I've been reading about how trees communicate through underground networks, sharing nutrients and warnings through fungal threads we barely understand. We talk about being connected, but we're not even sure what "connection" means in any real sense.

My neighbor waters her plants each morning at 7:14 exactly. I know this because I'm usually here, watching from behind the curtain I tell myself I'll close later. She has seven pots arranged on her fire escape, and she moves with the precision of someone who has created ritual around survival. Yesterday a police car idled across the street. Its lights painted everything red and blue and red again for thirty-seven minutes. She still came out at 7:14 carrying her tin cup of tea and the watering can that's probably as old as her.

There are sounds I can't identify coming from the apartment above—footsteps that don't match anyone's walk, furniture moving without explanation, a door closing that doesn't seem to lead anywhere when I stand in my bathroom and press my ear against the ceiling. But I don't question it. Maybe the building has its own rhythm, its own weather patterns that shift independent of the lives pressed within its bones.

I used to be afraid of growing older, thought it meant accumulating fewer possibilities. Now I suspect it might mean the opposite—that each year spent allows you to see more simultaneously. How morning and evening can exist in the same moment. How love can contain frustration without being diminished. How loneliness can feel companionable if you stop fighting it long enough to notice that it's speaking a language you're finally beginning to understand.

The book beside me fell open this morning to a page I didn't mark, words about time being more like ocean than river—but I hadn't intended to read about time today. I had thought about water instead, how it remembers its path despite obstacles, how it softens stone through persistence rather than force. How we are mostly fluid anyway, temporary arrangements of hydrogen and oxygen held together briefly and called human.

I keep meaning to call my sister, write letters to people who haven't heard from me in months, sort through the boxes that have been unpacking themselves slowly across various rooms for three years. But there's something seductive about living in this space between intention and action, where everything remains possible because nothing has been fully committed to. The coffee gets cold repeatedly. The light changes color. My neighbor tends her garden.

What am I writing about? Everything and nothing. Trying to catch the feeling of existing between certainties, where questions feel more honest than answers. Where the sound of rain begins suddenly outside, where I realize I started this not knowing what would emerge, where the word "emerge" seems suddenly perfect and strange on the page.

The phone has been off the hook most of today. The email sits unopened. There's a peace in this temporary withdrawal, like floating underwater where the surface noise becomes merely distant vibration. But now I'm hungry—hunger returning gradually like light after snow, which brings me back to bodies, to actual needs, to the immediate world requiring response.

I should go buy groceries. I should make coffee that I actually drink while warm. I should close the window before night brings different sounds. Tomorrow I'll call my sister and we'll speak about weather and work and the way our mother's hands shake now when she holds tea, about how we're all slowly becoming something we don't recognize yet, and how this might be the most interesting part of paying attention.
