The first time I saw bioluminescence I was shoulder-deep in July black water, convinced I was drowning until every motion tore open a new constellation. My panic became a galaxy—each kick a supernova, each wave a Milvian silk of cobalt stars. I laughed so hard I swallowed the bay, tasted salt and photoprotein, ancient chemistry married to present wonder. Later, wrapped in a towel that smelled of diesel and grandmother’s lavender, I realized awe is not an emotion but a transaction: give the world your small, trembling story; receive an ocean that answers back with light. Since then I measure years by the moments my body forgets its borders: a subway saxophone sustaining one blue note long enough to hush an entire car; finches threading a city park with arguments so quick they sound like one continuous sparkle; the crematorium clerk who handed back Dad’s ashes still warm, whispering “He did good, didn’t he?” I keep these receipts in the throat, small burning coins to spend when the news cycle insists the planet is bankrupt. Yesterday a kid on a scooter dragged a broken branch three blocks like a loyal dinosaur tail. Nobody corrected her. Instead we stepped aside, a quiet covenant: miracle passing, do not disturb. I want to live forever in that clearance, the hush widening until it fits every unsolved grief, every improbable tomorrow. I want to be the towel, the saxophone, the warm urn, the branch—anything that carries luminescence forward without asking for credit.
