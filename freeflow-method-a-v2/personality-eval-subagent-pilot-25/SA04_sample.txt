The wind up here doesn’t just blow; it hunts. It finds the gaps in your coat, the loose threads of your resolve, and it pulls at them until you feel unraveled. I’m standing at the edge of the old observatory, the stone floor cold enough through my boots to make me arch my toes. Below, the world is a patchwork quilt of grey and green, the town tucked into the valley like a secret someone forgot to tell.

The observatory itself is a relic. The great brass dome is tarnished to a dull bronze, spotted with lichen that looks like rust on a dying ship. Inside, the air smells of dust, old oil, and the sharp, metallic tang of abandonment. The telescope—the reason this place was built—points blindly at the ceiling now, its lens capped, a giant eye closed in a long, unbroken slumber. I wonder what it saw in its prime. Did it capture the birth of a star? Did it watch the silent, drifting dance of galaxies? Or did it just stare back at the emptiness, reflecting the loneliness of the person looking through the eyepiece?

We spend so much time looking up, don’t we? We think the answers are in the stars, in the infinite expanse of the cosmos. We think that if we can just find a planet that looks like ours, or understand the physics of a black hole, we’ll understand why we’re here. But maybe the answers aren't up there. Maybe they’re down here, in the dirt, in the way the wind howls through the cracks in the stone, in the quiet persistence of a weed growing through a concrete slab.

I run my hand along the railing. It’s rough, flaking paint catching on my skin. Below, the town is beginning to light up. One by one, tiny golden sparks ignite in the gathering dusk. I can imagine the people down there. Someone is cooking dinner, the smell of onions and garlic rising invisibly to meet me. Someone else is turning on a lamp, opening a book, laughing at a joke on the television. They are living their lives in a thousand tiny, separate universes, unaware that I am watching them from above, feeling like a ghost haunting a machine that stopped working years ago.

It’s strange how distance changes things. From up here, the cars are just beetles crawling along the grey ribbons of road. The arguments, the heartbreaks, the promotions, the failures—they all flatten out into a silent hum. The chaos of human life smooths into a landscape. It makes you feel insignificant, but in a way that is oddly comforting. If my mistakes are so small they can’t be seen from a thousand feet up, maybe they aren’t as heavy as I think they are.

The sun is dipping below the horizon now, a bruised purple melting into orange. The sky is that deep, impossible indigo that only exists for a few minutes a day. The birds have stopped singing; it’s just the wind and the distant, muffled roar of the highway.

I remember coming here as a child. My father brought me. He was a man of science, a man who believed in equations and certainty. He tried to explain the constellations to me, tracing the lines between stars with a laser pointer, his voice enthusiastic and loud in the quiet dark. I didn't care about the science. I just liked the feeling of being small. I liked the idea that the universe was so big it could hold anything—even my confusion, even my fear.

He’s gone now. His equations didn't save him, and neither did the stars. But standing here, I feel a little closer to him. Not because of the telescope, but because of the silence. He used to say that silence was just the space between things, the pause where the real thinking happened. I think he was right.

The first star pops out, sharp and defiant against the darkening blue. Venus, maybe. Or a distant sun that burned out a million years ago, its light just now reaching us, a ghost message from the past. It’s a beautiful, terrible thought. We are always looking at history. Even the present moment is gone the instant we recognize it.

I should go. The temperature is dropping, the kind of cold that settles in your bones and stays there. But I linger for a moment longer. I want to memorize this. The way the shadows stretch out from the trees like long, thin fingers. The smell of pine and cold stone. The feeling of being suspended between the earth and the sky, tethered to neither.

Eventually, I turn away from the railing. My footsteps echo hollowly on the metal stairs as I descend. The wind pushes at my back, as if urging me away, telling me this place belongs to the night now. I leave the observatory to its ghosts and its memories. I leave the telescope to its blind, silent vigil.

As I walk back toward the car, the lights of the town grow brighter, warmer. I can hear the faint sounds of life drifting up the hill—a dog barking, the low thrum of music. The world is waiting. It is messy and loud and confusing, but it is real. And for now, that is enough. I get in the car, turn the key, and the engine roars to life, drowning out the wind. I drive down the mountain, carrying the quiet of the stars inside me like a secret.
