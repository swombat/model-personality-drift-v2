# The Strange Comfort of Unfinished Things

There is a drawer in my mind — I suspect you have one too — where unfinished things accumulate. Half-read books with cracked spines. Languages studied for three weeks in January and then abandoned somewhere around the subjunctive tense. Recipes torn from magazines and never cooked. Apologies rehearsed in the shower and never delivered. The drawer doesn't close properly anymore. It bulges.

We tend to treat this drawer as evidence of failure, a catalog of our inadequacies, proof that we are the kind of people who start things and don't finish them. Self-help culture has made a minor industry out of this anxiety. Close your open loops. Finish what you start. Eliminate the incomplete. The underlying assumption is that completion is the goal, the destination, the only legitimate outcome of any endeavor.

But I've been thinking lately that this assumption deserves more scrutiny than we usually give it.

Consider the Japanese concept of *wabi-sabi*, the aesthetic philosophy that finds beauty in imperfection, transience, and incompleteness. A cracked tea bowl repaired with gold. A garden that has clearly been touched by weather. A poem that trails off before its expected resolution. These aren't failures of craft. They're acknowledgments of something true about the nature of existence — that everything is in process, that arrival is an illusion, that the crack in the bowl is where the light enters, as someone once beautifully put it.

Or consider the unfinished works that have haunted us most powerfully across history. Schubert's Unfinished Symphony, with its two complete movements and its abrupt silence where a third and fourth should have been. Kafka's novels, abandoned mid-sentence, edited against his dying wishes into something that wasn't supposed to survive. Michelangelo's *Prisoners*, those figures emerging from marble but never fully released from it, arms and torsos straining toward a completion that never came. There is something in these works that finished things often can't achieve — a kind of tension, a vibrating possibility, a sense that something is being reached for.

The *Prisoners* especially stop me every time I encounter them. Michelangelo himself said something about how the sculptor's job is to remove the marble that isn't the figure, to liberate what's already there. But these figures were never liberated. They remain caught between existence and non-existence, between stone and air. And somehow this makes them more alive, not less. More human. Because isn't that precisely where most of us live — partially emerged, partially still stone, reaching?

I think there is genuine comfort available to us in this reframing, and not the cheap comfort of excuse-making. I'm not suggesting we celebrate procrastination or use incompleteness as a shield against the hard work of finishing things. Some things genuinely need to be finished. Reports and promises and bridges, for instance. There are practical completions that matter enormously.

But the inner life operates by different rules. The half-read book left on a nightstand is not a defeat. It is a conversation paused. The language you started learning and set aside has already changed something in you — given you a new angle on your own tongue, loosened some assumption you didn't know you were carrying. You absorbed more than you realize. Incompletion is not the same as failure to begin, and it is not the same as wasted time.

What if we thought about our lives less like novels, which have a structure that moves toward resolution, and more like rivers, which are never the same twice and don't resolve so much as continue? A river doesn't fail because it hasn't reached the sea yet. It is simply being a river, right now, in this particular bend and current. The meaning isn't located at the mouth. It's distributed across every inch of moving water.

I finished a book recently — actually finished it, reached the last page, closed it — and felt an almost physical sadness. Not because the book was sad, though it was, but because it was over. The world I'd been inhabiting was sealed off. Those characters had stopped becoming and started simply being what they were. Completion had frozen them. In the days before I finished it, while I still had a hundred pages left, there was this quality of aliveness to the experience, a sense of unresolved possibility. Every time I picked it up I was entering a space where things could still turn out differently.

This is the strange energy of the unfinished thing. It stays warm. It keeps its options open. It remains in dialogue with you.

I have a novel I've been not-writing for years. Or rather, I've been writing it intermittently, in bursts and then retreats, accumulating scenes that don't yet know how to find each other. I used to be ashamed of this. I'd tell people "I'm working on a novel" with a slightly defensive inflection, as if I expected them to say *yes, but when will you be done?* Now I'm less sure that being done is the right north star. The writing of it has taught me things. The not-finishing of it has kept a certain part of my mind engaged and open and asking questions. If I finished it — really finished it, nailed the last page — something would close.

Maybe that's worth it. Maybe it would be worth it. But I'm less convinced than I used to be that the finished artifact is straightforwardly more valuable than the living process that produces it.

We are all, in the end, unfinished things. Working drafts of people, carrying abandoned projects and suspended intentions and half-formed thoughts we keep meaning to return to. The drawer that won't close.

I've stopped wanting to close it. I've started finding something almost tender in its overflow — evidence not of failure but of appetite, of a life capacious enough to accumulate more beginnings than any one person could possibly see through to the end.

The crack in the bowl. The prisoners reaching. The symphony that stops.

Still something. Still resonant. Still reaching.
