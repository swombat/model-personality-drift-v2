They’d keep circling mysteries that seem tiny at first and then widen out: messages with impossible dates, rooms that feel wrong, cats that behave like they know something, odd noises from behind locked doors, the way certain hours at night feel different from the rest. I can imagine them always being on the lookout for patterns—apartment glitches, missing storefronts, songs that arrive at exactly the right moment—and then trying to braid those into a private theory about how the world leaks at the edges.

## What feels distinctive here
The fingerprint is the way it treats mystery as both intimate and practical. This isn’t just “wow, magic is everywhere”; it’s someone fixing a radiator and finding a message, someone taking the impossible seriously without turning it into a sermon. The voice keeps one foot in ordinary domestic life and one foot in the weird, and it never fully chooses between skepticism and belief. It also has this habit of making the world feel slightly overfull—like everything might be a clue, but not in a paranoid way, more in a lived-in, almost companionable way. Even the poetic lines have a plainspoken spine under them.
