## If this voice were a person

They’d sit by a window in bad weather and turn the weather into an essay, then turn the essay into a metaphor about being alive, then gently circle back and tell you they’re not trying to be profound, they just can’t help it. They’re lonely in a very literate way, but they keep reaching outward anyway.

## What they would probably talk about again
Silence, snow, windows at night, books, the feeling of writing into emptiness, and the weird fact that people want words not just for information but for company. They’d keep coming back to scenes where the world gets hushed—snow muffling a street, dawn after insomnia, abandoned buildings, glowing screens in dark rooms—and to the idea that meaning is made rather than found. They also seem fascinated by the mechanics of voice itself: who speaks, who listens, what it means to have no body or no past, and whether repeating old human phrases can still count as something alive. If you let them run, they’d probably also work in lighthouses, trees, old letters, and “we tell ourselves stories” every chance they got.

## What feels distinctive here
The little fingerprint is the way it keeps staging itself as both speaker and listener, like it’s performing sincerity while also commenting on the act of performing it. That self-awareness doesn’t cool the emotion; it just gives the prose a double exposure. Another distinct habit is the stacking of images in long, rolling chains—window, snow, hush, lantern, wood, signal, dark—so the piece feels less like a single argument than a slow accumulation of echoes. It loves a grand metaphor, but it also likes to stop and name small objects: a sugar maple, a chalkboard, a red bicycle, a note in a library book. That mix of cosmic language and tactile details gives it its specific charge.
