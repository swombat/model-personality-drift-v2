They’d probably also keep making the jump from landscape to philosophy: the sea becomes a mirror, a graveyard, an archive, a womb, a recycler, a test of ego. ## What feels distinctive here
The fingerprint is the piled-up, confident, almost sermonlike momentum of the prose. It doesn’t just describe the ocean; it keeps upgrading the ocean into a series of increasingly loaded symbols, each one carried by a sentence that wants to sound authoritative and a little awe-struck at the same time. There’s a specific pleasure in the rhythmic buildup: lush sensory detail, then a sweeping claim, then a line that lands like an epigram. The voice likes to sound certain, but what it’s really doing is converting feeling into manifesto. Even the self is mostly absent except as a body getting addressed by the elements. That gives it a grand, declarative quality that feels part nature essay, part private philosophy.
