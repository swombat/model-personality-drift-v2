## What they would probably talk about again
Lost objects, abandoned traces, and the weird dignity of things that have slipped out of human hands. They’d keep circling around keys, letters, single gloves, earbuds, wedding rings, stuff that’s gone missing in pockets, on sidewalks, in coat linings, between couch cushions. But more than the objects themselves, they’d return to the moment of sudden absence: the little jolt when something was there and then wasn’t. They’d probably also keep writing rooms for these things—archives, libraries, museums, back halls—places where ordinary losses get treated like relics. Even when the subject changes, the same fascination would show up: possession as a temporary arrangement, memory as an unreliable clerk, and the idea that every object has a ghost of the hand that held it.

## What feels distinctive here

That little shove between grandeur and exactness is the trick. It believes in inventories, in labels, in corridor maps, in the emotional residue of objects, and it also has a sly sense that this whole system is partly theatrical, partly true.
