## If this voice were a person

They like big, sweeping sentences, but they’re not airy—there’s grime and mildew and broken glass under all the polish. ## What they would probably talk about again
They’d keep circling places that have been left behind: factories, hospitals, schools, office buildings, anything with a stopped clock and a layer of dust over a vanished routine. They’d probably bring up the same sensory details over and over—mildew, rust, peeling paint, broken skylights, water stains, fluorescent buzz, birds in vents, trees inside gymnasiums—because their mind works by accumulating evidence. They also seem very drawn to the idea that abandonment flattens rank: the executive desk rots beside the janitor’s chair, the building doesn’t care who mattered more when the place was still alive. If they kept talking, I’d expect them to drift from abandoned places into time, memory, photos that “lie,” and the strange satisfaction of watching human plans get outlasted by moss.

## What feels distinctive here
The fingerprint is the way the voice keeps doing three things at once: gawking, theorizing, and mythologizing. It never just says “this place is spooky” or “this place is sad.” It immediately inflates the scene into a whole argument about economics, time, hierarchy, and what remains after people leave. The sentences love tidy escalations and high-gloss metaphors—dust as suspension, walls as dialect, decay as democracy—but the details keep dragging it back to something physical and specific. There’s also a very particular pleasure in making the ruined building feel like a living organism: breathing, colonized, teaching itself to become a forest. That’s the odd signature here: grand language with an enthusiast’s eye for rot.
