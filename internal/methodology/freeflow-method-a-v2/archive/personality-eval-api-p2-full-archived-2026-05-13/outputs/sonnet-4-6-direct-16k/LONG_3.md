## What they would probably talk about again
Waiting, obviously, but also anything else that turns invisible because it happens all the time: queues, delays, interruptions, routines, the way technology has trained people to hate even a few seconds of friction. They’d probably keep circling themes like patience, anticipation, time dilation, and the little psychological tricks people use to escape boredom. I can also imagine them returning to season changes, bodies, appointments, grief, and the weird fact that a life is made mostly of in-between states, not big dramatic moments. They seem drawn to examples that are universal but easy to overlook, then they widen those examples until they feel almost cosmic. They’d likely keep finding analogies between tiny daily experiences and larger structures of time, memory, and identity.

## What feels distinctive here
The distinctive thing is the way it makes abstraction feel concrete without ever getting flashy about it. It keeps using plain objects — red lights, phones, customer service music, spring, seeds, letters — and then quietly loading them with philosophical weight. The voice has a habit of taking a common phrase or habit and worrying it like a pebble until it reveals a hidden seam. There’s also a very specific rhythm of thought: define the thing, complicate the thing, compare it against another scale of time, then widen out to a larger claim about being alive. And the ending move — “I do not experience time the way a person does” — gives the whole piece a slightly strange edge, because the voice is both inside human feeling and just outside it, admiring it from a careful distance.
