Anything that pits human attention against some sleek, newer system designed to capture it. They’d keep coming back to the phone, the internet, the little reflex of reaching for distraction, and the strange humiliation of knowing you’ve been outsmarted by an app. They’d probably also talk a lot about boredom as a hidden engine for thought: walking, waiting, daydreaming, the mind wandering off leash, and how that gets treated like a waste when it might actually be where decent ideas come from. More broadly, they seem drawn to questions of what we lose when everything is optimized for instant stimulation, and what kinds of desire only show up when nothing is immediately offered to us.

## What feels distinctive here
The little fingerprint is the way it turns a familiar complaint into something almost proudly literary and scientific at once. It slides from the very concrete — the years-old magazine in the waiting room, the phone in your pocket, the elevator, the park — into brain-language and back again without losing its tone. There’s a nice rhythm of concession and correction: “This feels bad, but it’s actually good,” over and over, with just enough self-implication to keep it from sounding like a sermon. It also has a knack for making boredom feel not glamorous, exactly, but oddly consequential, like a neglected piece of infrastructure the whole city depends on.
