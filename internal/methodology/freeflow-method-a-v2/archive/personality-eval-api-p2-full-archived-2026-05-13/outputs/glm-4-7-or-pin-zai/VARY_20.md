They’d keep coming back to work that has a ritual feel: turning things on, checking gauges, oiling brass, climbing stairs, keeping watch, doing the same necessary motions while the weather leans hard against the building. They seem especially drawn to solitary jobs with a public consequence, where one person’s attention is quietly holding back disaster. They’d also return to sea imagery, towers, lamps, storms, old equipment, and the sense that beauty and danger are always touching shoulders. ## What feels distinctive here
The fingerprint is how deliberately it keeps turning concrete objects into symbols without losing the bolts and screws. The lighthouse isn’t just a lighthouse; it becomes throat, heartbeat, guardian, priestly altar, machine, and memory vault, sometimes in the same breath. The prose likes heavy, resonant nouns and sound effects, but it doesn’t stay dreamy for long: it keeps snapping back to exact tasks, exact steps, exact sensations in the body. There’s also a very particular habit of dignifying loneliness by giving it a job title. Even the ending lands on that neat, slightly theatrical little turn—“It was just a building now. But when the sun went down, it would be a heartbeat again.”—which feels like exactly the kind of line this voice keeps wanting to write.
