Anything that lets them think about distance without having to name the original pain too directly. They’d probably write about cities reached by accident, the emotional residue of family or childhood holidays, and the idea that movement doesn’t solve anything so much as changes the shape of what you can bear to look at.

## What feels distinctive here
The fingerprint is the way the voice keeps slipping from plain travel prose into slightly theatrical, almost antique-seeming comparison without losing its footing. It loves one sharp, memorable image after another—the seed shells as “a tiny storm in miniature,” the train as a “wounded animal,” the telegram, the dogs sighing humanly—and it uses those images to make the whole piece feel both intimate and curated. There’s also a very particular habit of making wisdom sound half-found, half-invented, as if the narrator is discovering their own beliefs in real time. The result is not raw diary energy; it’s more like someone composing memory into a portable myth, with just enough irony to keep the sentiment from spilling over.
