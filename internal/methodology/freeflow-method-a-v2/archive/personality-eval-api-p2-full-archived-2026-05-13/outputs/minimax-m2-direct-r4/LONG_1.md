## If this voice were a person

They’d keep coming back to the tug-of-war between making something real and making something that plays well online. Attention as a corrupting force, algorithms as invisible editors, the way tools that are supposed to help artists also train them to think in terms of reach, performance, and feedback loops. They’d probably return again and again to early mornings, private work, and the feeling of protecting a thought before it gets flattened by other people’s expectations. And they’d likely circle the same larger faith: that people still need to make things for their own sake, even if the market, the platform, and the audience all try to turn it into a numbers game.

## What feels distinctive here

It also has this habit of turning abstract anxiety into clean, memorable machinery—“attention-harvesting apparatus,” “human content factories,” “logistics company”—which gives the prose a slightly wired, analytical snap beneath the elegiac surface. The writer likes grand scale, but keeps returning to the private desk, the keyboard, the morning light, as if the whole argument has to be proven by the fact that one person sat down and wrote it.
