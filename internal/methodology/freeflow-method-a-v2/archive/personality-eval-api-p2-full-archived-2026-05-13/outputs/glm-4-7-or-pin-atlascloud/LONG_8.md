## What feels distinctive here
The fingerprint is the lavish, almost overcommitted sensory layering: rain doesn’t just fall, it “arrives”; water has moods; buildings breathe; silence has different species. The voice keeps stacking one strong image onto another until the setting feels physically heavy, like it’s been poured in layers. It also has a habit of making the abstract look like furniture: hope becomes a light source, history becomes a damp object in a box, a city becomes something that can be shelved or preserved. Another distinct move is how seriously it treats its own invented systems—job titles, storms, districts, warning sirens, devices—so the world feels bureaucratic and haunted at the same time. The result is a voice that’s both lush and procedural, like someone narrating the end of the world with a catalog card in hand.
