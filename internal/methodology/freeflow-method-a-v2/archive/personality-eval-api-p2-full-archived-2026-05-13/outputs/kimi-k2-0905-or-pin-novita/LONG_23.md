## If this voice were a person
This feels like someone who can’t help turning life into a sentence with a second life inside it. They’re bookish, theatrical, and very fluent in self-mythology, but not in a smug way so much as in the way of someone who’s been alone with their thoughts for too long and has learned to make them sparkle. There’s a swagger to the voice, but it’s mixed with self-mockery and a kind of nervous appetite: for sex, for cities, for languages, for being changed by whatever happens next. ## What they would probably talk about again
Again and again, this voice would come back to places where appetite and disappointment touch: cheap apartments, borrowed rooms, trains, markets, odd encounters with strangers, and the small rituals that make upheaval feel narratable. They’d keep circling fruit, bruising, ripeness, and the idea that damage can become flavor if you wait long enough. They’d also keep talking about cities as if they were flirtations with a long memory—Paris especially, but really any place with transit, weather, and enough surfaces to bounce language off of. Expect more stories where someone teaches them a word, kisses them, leaves with a borrowed belt, or says something that sounds like a thesis line and ends up governing the next year of their life.

## What feels distinctive here
The fingerprint is the way the prose keeps yoking together high theory and bodily mess without ever making that feel like a gimmick. It can go from Barthes to a condom wrapper to a priest translating “I love” into Arabic to a boar in a doorway, and somehow the voice stays in one piece. It likes compressed little aphorisms that sound discovered rather than invented, but it also loves a good comic sidestep—“a tomato that had been insulted by the century” is exactly the kind of joke that reveals the whole engine. The voice is at its most itself when it treats translation, bruising, drift, and hunger as if they’re all versions of the same problem.
