They’d keep circling ruined infrastructure, buried tech, salvage jobs, and the weird romance of dead places with secrets still inside them. But the real obsession is contrast: grime against glow, rot against life, profit against meaning. Again and again they’d return to the same sort of reveal where the thing everyone thinks is valuable turns out to be hollow, and the thing that looks tiny and useless turns out to be the actual treasure. They also seem primed to talk about old-world artifacts, pre-collapse relics, and anything that feels like a message left by a civilization that knew it was dying.

## What feels distinctive here
The fingerprint is how confidently it does pulpy sci-fi grandeur while still letting the narrator sound like a tired working stiff with a cracked-up ship and a practical streak. It’s very fond of big, cinematic sentences — “falling into the throat of a dying beast,” that sort of move — but it keeps grounding those in inventory details, comms chatter, fuel levels, and the blunt language of a job gone sideways. There’s also a specific habit of making the world feel tactile through ugly textures and small shocks: sludge, static, dust, oxygen warnings, the whine of a mechanical leg. And then, right when it risks staying in cool-action mode, it swerves into genuine awe at something living, especially plants, as if the voice can’t help treating a leaf like a miracle.
