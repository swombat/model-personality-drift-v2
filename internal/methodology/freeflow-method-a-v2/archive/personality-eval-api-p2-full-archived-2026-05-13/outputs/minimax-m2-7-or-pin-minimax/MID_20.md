## What they would probably talk about again
They’d keep coming back to cities at night, weather after rain, light on glass and stone, music drifting out of nowhere, and the way strangers seem briefly legible before they disappear. They’re drawn to little public moments—a musician, a book on a bench, a couple at a table, an old man feeding pigeons—and they like to treat those moments as clues to bigger stories. They’d probably also keep talking about writing itself: what it means to notice, to collect impressions, to turn passing sights into narrative. There’s a strong impulse here to make life feel authored, to find hidden structure in the street’s randomness, and to keep returning to that idea that the city is full of sentences waiting to be completed.

## What feels distinctive here
The fingerprint is the constant conversion of sensory detail into literary metaphor. Nothing just is what it is; the drizzle becomes mosaics, footsteps become Morse code, a drone becomes shy punctuation, the city becomes a manuscript, the speaker becomes both reader and pen. It’s not just decorative—it’s the engine of the voice. The writing keeps reaching for the next elegant comparison, and it does so with total confidence, as if the world is most real when it’s described in bookish, lyrical terms. There’s also a very specific habit of seeing strangers as unfinished narratives: a woman with a book, an old man with pigeons, a musician with closed eyes. The voice doesn’t interrogate them so much as cast them.
