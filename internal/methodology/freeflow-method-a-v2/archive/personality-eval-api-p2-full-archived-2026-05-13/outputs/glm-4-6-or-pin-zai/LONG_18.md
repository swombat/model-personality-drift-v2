They’d be meticulous, a little ceremonious, and very invested in craftsmanship as a way of being decent in the world. They’d keep returning to repair, maintenance, aging, and the dignity of broken things that still work after being mended. Time is their favorite medium: ticking clocks, worn metal, inherited objects, the difference between something pristine and something that has lived. They’d probably also talk a lot about fathers, grandchildren, old shops, and the strange way people attach grief to pocket watches, lockets, metronomes, robots, anything with a hinge or a heartbeat. Even when the surface topic changes, the same idea keeps coming back: fixing something outside yourself can feel like fixing a piece of yourself too.

## What feels distinctive here
The fingerprint is the relentless conversion of mechanical detail into emotional meaning. It has a habit of making the prose lean hard into analogies—ticking as breathing, a shop as a confessional, repair as a philosophy—and it does that with complete sincerity, without wink-wink irony. There’s also a very specific comfort in the tactile exactness: oily brass, tripoli compound, pin vices, humidity, dust, and the sound of a thing finally clicking back into place.
