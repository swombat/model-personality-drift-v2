## If this voice were a person
This feels like someone who can’t help turning every feeling into a little machine with a lever and a cost. They’re drawn to big, cinematic images—the lighthouse, the fog, the blue beam, the mirror—but they keep snapping the scene back to procedure: gauges, fuel, logs, steps, maintenance. That gives them a strange mix of grandeur and practicality, like a person who wants to tell you a myth but also really wants to make sure you know where the pressure line is. They seem emotionally serious, but not gushy; more like they’re trying to make loss legible by giving it parts, names, and a system. There’s a soft spot in them for what gets forgotten, but they approach it with a clean, almost mechanical sadness.

## What they would probably talk about again
They’d keep returning to the problem of what things are worth sacrificing to keep something alive: memory, identity, comfort, maybe the self. They like images of preservation that are actually forms of consumption—light running on nostalgia, a machine that needs the past as fuel, a keeper who has to erase herself to keep others safe. I’d expect them to keep writing about lost objects, old songs, half-remembered people, weather that feels alive, and jobs that are less heroic than they sound. They’d probably also come back to the idea that a place can be lonely but still occupied by duty, with the job itself becoming the only stable thing left.

## What feels distinctive here
The fingerprint is how hard the prose leans into sensory overdesign while staying emotionally blunt. It keeps stacking exact textures and colors—rough wool, heated brass, electric blue, rusted handlebars, ozone, old rain—then undercuts the fantasy with plain-language bookkeeping. The other signature move is that final twist of self-erasure: memory becomes literal fuel, and the character treats that like paperwork. It’s not just “sad magical realism”; it’s a very specific habit of making the abstract cost of keeping things going into a physical machine you can point at.
