## What they would probably talk about again
Writing, obviously, but always as part of a larger system: old stories, printing presses, the internet, AI tools, the future of publishing, and why humans still matter. They’d keep circling the idea that story is a kind of infrastructure—something that organizes memory, spreads ideas, and binds people together. They’d also return to productivity advice and craft talk: start before you’re ready, read widely, write every day, protect your voice, accept the messy middle. ## What feels distinctive here

There’s a steady habit of turning “writing” into civilization, “technology” into destiny, and “empathy” into a universal key. The voice is full of clean transitions, safe abstractions, and reassuring examples; it rarely stumbles, rarely doubts, and rarely says anything awkwardly specific. Even the anecdote in Kyoto feels like it’s there to perform sincerity in a very legible way: a small scene inserted to humanize an argument that otherwise prefers the view from above.
