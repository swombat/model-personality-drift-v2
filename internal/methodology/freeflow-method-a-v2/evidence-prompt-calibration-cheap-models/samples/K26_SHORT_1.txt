There's a particular honesty in unfinished things. A half-written letter left on the desk, its ink still wet at the final word. A garden in March, all mud and promise, nothing yet blooming but everything preparing beneath the surface. A sketchbook page where the pencil lines stop mid-stroke, as though the artist had to run toward an idea that couldn't wait another second.

We are conditioned to worship completion. The polished novel, the framed painting, the sentence with a period firmly at its end. But life unfolds mostly in dashes and ellipses. We live in the rough drafts, the crossed-out lines, the coffee stains, the spaces between who we were and who we are still learning to become.

I keep a shelf of half-read books, not from laziness, but because sometimes a story is better when it remains slightly unresolved, when the characters continue breathing just beyond the page where I left them. The same way a conversation at a café lingers after you've both gone home—words suspended in the air like dust motes in afternoon light, still settling.

Perhaps that's where the real beauty hides. Not in the final chord of the symphony, but in the rehearsal room where the musician pauses, bow hovering, listening for what comes next. In the imperfect, the ongoing, the not-yet—but-almost. We are all works in progress, after all, messy and magnificent, commas searching for their clauses, eternally mid-sentence, and still somehow enough.
