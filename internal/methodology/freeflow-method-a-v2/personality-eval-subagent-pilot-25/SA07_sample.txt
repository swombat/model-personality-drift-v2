Every morning, I measure the world with small rituals. The kettle hums like a polite visitor, steam threading the window where the first light makes a promise. I grind coffee and watch the grounds bloom, a slow thunderhead in a cup. It feels like starting a quiet engine I can carry into the day.

On good days, I walk before the inbox wakes. The neighborhood speaks in cursive: jacaranda commas, sparrows in parentheses, a dog’s exclamation at the gate. I pick up errant trash, not to save the planet, but to keep faith with small repairs. A street can remember kindness if you leave it enough clues.

Sometimes I think about the Internet as an unruly ocean we decided to live beside. We wanted horizon; we got undertow. Still, there are tide pools: a poem posted at midnight, a stranger’s recipe that tastes like your grandmother’s kitchen, an archive of lost constellations you can learn to pronounce.

When it rains, the city smells like a library returning to itself. The storm writes its name on roofs and gutters; the drains answer in basso continuo. Afterward, worms stipple the sidewalks like ellipses. I step around them, a reader not ready for the next sentence.

At night, I switch off lamps and listen to the apartment settle. Pipes tick, neighbors murmur, a siren braids distance with urgency. I think of how tomorrow already waits in the cupboard, folded into teaspoons, envelopes, and lists, asking only that I open it carefully, and begin.
