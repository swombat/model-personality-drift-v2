I find myself thinking about silence, and how rare it has become. Not just the absence of sound, but a particular quality of internal stillness. We are in the golden age of noise—not just auditory noise, but informational, emotional, and algorithmic noise. Our pockets buzz with the anxieties of the world, the performative joys of acquaintances, and the distilled outrage of strangers. We are forever in a crowded room, even when we are alone.

I was on a train recently, and a sudden, inexplicable tunnel severed all signals. For three minutes, the glowing rectangles around me dimmed into black mirrors. People looked up, startled, as if waking from a collective hypnosis. Some made awkward eye contact and smiled. One person simply stared out the window at the blurring darkness, her own faint reflection superimposed on the rushing stone. In that void, there was a palpable shift. The low-grade tension of being "plugged in" evaporated. It was a forced silence, a pocket of digital vacuum, and it felt… luxurious.

It made me wonder what we are cultivating in the constant hum. Our minds are like rivers; they shape themselves to the channels we provide. If the channel is a rapid-fire stream of fragmented stimuli—a clip of a war, a meme, a friend’s vacation, a work email, a tragic headline—our thought patterns become fragmented in response. We start to crave the jumps, the cuts, the dopamine spikes of novelty and resolution. Deep, sustained thought requires a different topography: meanders, slow pools, eddies where ideas can mix and settle.

I miss boredom. Not the dull, oppressive kind, but the fertile boredom of childhood—staring at a ceiling, watching dust motes dance in a sunbeam, letting the mind wander without destination or deadline. From that "empty" space, entire imaginary worlds would generate. Now, at the first twinge of boredom, we reach for the device. We’ve outsourced our inner generative capacity to an endless feed of pre-generated content.

There’s a paradox: we are more connected than ever, yet I sense a growing loneliness of a specific type. It’s not a loneliness from being unseen, but from being perpetually *viewed* without being *known*. We curate and consume curated lives, and the authentic, messy, quiet self—the self that exists in the unobserved moments—can feel like a ghost haunting its own life.

So, I am making small rebellions. I leave my phone in another room for an hour. I sit with a cup of tea and do nothing but watch the light change. I try to read a physical book until my mind quiets down enough to follow the thread. It’s difficult. The itch to "check" is powerful, a neurological siren call. But in the resistance, there are glimpses of that old silence. And in that silence, something begins to stir. Not a tweet, not a hot take, not a reaction—but a thought, slow and tentative, rising from its own depths, like a fish coming up to the still surface of a pond.
