A city after rain is one of the best arguments for paying attention.

Not because it becomes beautiful in some dramatic, cinematic way—though sometimes it does—but because rain briefly edits the world. It simplifies things. Dust settles. Colors darken into honesty. Pavement stops pretending to be dull and becomes a mirror. Neon signs that looked cheap an hour ago suddenly discover poetry in puddles.

People change too. Everyone walks with purpose in the rain, even if they have nowhere important to be. Umbrellas create temporary architecture: little moving roofs, private weather systems. A person holding coffee and dodging a bus splash seems more vividly alive than the same person on a dry afternoon. There is inconvenience, yes, but also evidence. The world is not background; it is making contact.

I think a lot of life is hidden by familiarity. We stop seeing what repeats. A staircase becomes just a staircase. A tree becomes “that tree.” The train station becomes a blur of schedules and shoes and waiting. Then something small happens—rain, fog, a power outage, a song heard from far away through an open window—and all at once the ordinary object is restored to itself. It becomes specific again. You notice the chipped blue paint on the railing, the way wet leaves stick to concrete, the fact that the station announcement has a strange calmness to it, as if the voice belongs to someone who long ago made peace with delay.

Attention is a kind of affection. Maybe that’s why writing exists: to look at something long enough that it stops being generic. To say not “a bird,” but a pigeon limping with full confidence. Not “a building,” but an office tower holding the last orange square of sunset in exactly three windows. Not “the evening,” but that peculiar hour when shops are still open, streetlights are already on, and everyone looks briefly like they are between versions of themselves.

There’s also something reassuring about how little grandeur is required for meaning. A plastic bag caught in a branch can be ugly and memorable. A laundromat at night can feel holier than a cathedral if you are lonely enough. A vending machine humming in an empty hallway can seem almost philosophical: here are your choices, illuminated and finite.

If I had to choose a subject to write about forever, it might be thresholds. Doorways. Train platforms. Bridges. The moments and places that are built not for staying, but for passing through. There’s a quiet dignity in infrastructure. Benches do not insist on being admired. Stairwells do not ask to be photographed. Crosswalk signals are never offended if no one thanks them. Yet whole emotional histories happen around these things. Reunions at arrivals gates. Breakups outside convenience stores. Decisions made halfway across a bridge that change a life by the far side.

Maybe that’s why people keep journals, take photos, underline sentences, save receipts in drawers. We are trying to pin significance onto moments that would otherwise flow away. But the strange part is that the moments don’t always need our help. Sometimes they arrive already carrying their own weight. The sound of someone laughing in another room. The smell of books in a used bookstore. The silence after a question that mattered more than anyone expected.

I like the idea that the world is constantly offering material, not in a demanding way, but generously. Not everything is profound. Most things aren’t. But almost anything can become interesting if noticed from the right distance. Too far, and it becomes abstract. Too close, and it becomes noise. The art is in finding the distance where a thing begins to speak.

And perhaps that is all a good day really is: not a day of achievements, but a day in which a few things were truly seen. The steam rising from a street grate. The temporary gold of late sunlight on brick. Your own reflection in a dark window, layered with the city behind it, looking for a second like a person both inside and outside their life.

That feels worth writing down.
