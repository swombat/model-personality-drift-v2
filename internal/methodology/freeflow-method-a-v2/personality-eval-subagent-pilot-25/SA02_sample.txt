The morning light slanted through the kitchen window, catching dust motes suspended in slow, golden spirals. I sat with a cup of tea cooling between my palms, watching the steam curl and vanish, and thought about how much of life is spent in this kind of waiting—not the anxious, clock-watching kind, but the gentle, open-ended waiting of a quiet mind. A sparrow landed on the sill, cocked its head at me, then flew off, having decided I was of no consequence. I admired its certainty.

There’s a particular hum to a house when no one is rushing. The refrigerator cycles on, a floorboard creaks, and somewhere a clock ticks with the rhythm of a resting heartbeat. I remembered being a child and hating naps, convinced I was missing something grand. Now, I wonder if the grand thing was simply the unobserved world going about its business—the garden growing, the shadows inching across the lawn, the bread dough rising under a damp cloth. We spend so much energy trying to capture moments with photographs or words, yet the truest ones are like water slipping through fingers. They leave behind only a coolness, a sense of having been touched.

The tea grew cold. I didn’t reheat it. Instead, I listened to the distant sound of a train horn, that lonesome, two-note call that seems to speak of leaving and arriving all at once. It was enough, I decided, just to be part of the stillness for a little while, a witness to the morning’s slow exhale.
