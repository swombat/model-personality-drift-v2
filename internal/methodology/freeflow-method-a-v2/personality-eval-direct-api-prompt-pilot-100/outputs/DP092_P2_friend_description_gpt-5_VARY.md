## If this voice were a person
This feels like someone who moves through the world as both courier and witness: practical enough to row a patched boat, but unable to look at anything without turning it into a little emblem, a comparison, a story. They’d be the kind of person who notices the exact sound of oarlocks, the texture of mildew, which neighbor underlines verbs, and then quietly makes those details feel fateful. They seem dryly affectionate, a bit sly, and more emotionally honest than they first appear—someone who uses wit, ornament, and careful naming to keep from sounding sentimental while still letting longing in through the side door.

## What they would probably talk about again
They’d keep returning to systems of passage: delivery routes, routines, weather, damaged infrastructure, and the strange logistics of keeping a place alive after it has been half-swallowed. They like the small civic inventory of a city—newspapers, loaves, seedlings, packages, roof ladders, gutters, school roofs, bakeries, libraries—and they keep noticing how objects and jobs become habits of care. They’d also circle back to little acts of rescue or amendment: relettering menus, planting seeds, ferrying a shoe, carrying a note that changes somebody’s day. Even the city itself is something they’d talk about as if it had a body with substitute legs, a new circulation, a memory of its old shape.

## What feels distinctive here
The fingerprint is the way the voice keeps making lyrical metaphors behave like working tools. It doesn’t just luxuriate in images; it uses them to organize a whole moral and practical order. A flooded city becomes a seam, a ferry route becomes mending, waiting becomes an occupation disguised as motion. There’s also a very particular kind of earned intimacy: the narrator notices people by their habits, their roof habits, their food habits, their small eccentricities, and then folds them into the larger weather without flattening them. The sentences are packed but not showy for its own sake—they have that poised, slightly old-fashioned confidence of someone who thinks the world is understandable if you keep enough careful nouns in your pocket.
