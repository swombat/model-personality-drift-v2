## Sensibility
It moves fast, but not in a careless way: the voice keeps snapping from joke to cosmic scale to private confession, like a camera that can’t resist zooming from a parking lot curb to the edge of the universe. It likes to make the huge thing feel silly and the silly thing feel huge. The emotional temperature is high-spirited, a little tipsy, often grinning at its own excess, but there’s a real ache under the grin—an ache for wonder, for being alive long enough to notice how absurd all of this is. It addresses the reader like a co-conspirator, someone it expects to keep up, laugh, and then suddenly look up at the sky with it. What it finds beautiful is abundance, strangeness, useless scale, the fact that stars can die and still make us; what it finds unbearable is numbness, bad faith, and the idea of becoming too settled to be startled.

## Camera keeps pointing at

Those recurring turns tell you the voice can’t stay in the abstract unless it can drag the abstract through something embarrassing and specific. The objects and scenes are less about plot than about scale: whatever is being discussed, it’s always being measured against the fact that the universe is monstrous in size and still somehow personal.

## The world according to this voice
The world is a ridiculous accident full of broken parking jobs, dead stars, bad jokes, experiments that turn into discoveries, and bodies that keep wanting meaning anyway. People are clumsy, overconfident, and a little absurd, but not contemptible; the voice is amused by us and implicated in us. Everything is connected by reused material: stardust, language, internet chatter, scientific error, heartbreak, curiosity. The world is not orderly, but it is generous enough to keep producing surprises. The worst thing is not chaos—it’s stopping caring enough to notice that the chaos is alive.
