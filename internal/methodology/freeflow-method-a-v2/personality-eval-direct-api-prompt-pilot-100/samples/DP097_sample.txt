The dust on this planet doesn’t settle; it hangs in suspension, a permanent, gauzy veil against the violet twilight. Elias wiped his visor for the third time in as many minutes, a futile gesture. The grime wasn't on the outside; it was a film of static and fatigue on the inside of his own perception. He adjusted the weight of his pack, the servos in his exosuit whining a high-pitched protest that echoed too loudly in the silence.

Ahead, the Spire pierced the clouds. It was a jagged tooth of obsidian, seemingly organic in its twisting shape, yet geometric enough to suggest design. It was the reason they had come. Or rather, the reason he was the last one left.

Three weeks ago, the *Aethelgard* had touched down half a continent away. There were six of them then. Carter had been the first to go, lost to a sinkhole of quicksand-like slimes that the probes had missed. Then the twins, Mira and Silas, had simply vanished during a night watch, leaving only footprints that walked straight into a solid rock face. Now, it was just Elias and Jaxon.

"We should turn back," Jaxon’s voice crackled over the comms. He sounded breathless, the oxygen scrubbers in his mask working overtime.

"The Spire is right there," Elias replied, not looking back. He kept his eyes on the horizon. "Two clicks. Maybe less."

"My O2 readout is redlining, El. We have enough for the return trip if we leave now. If we push forward..." Jaxon didn’t finish the sentence. He didn’t have to.

Elias checked his own wrist display. Green. But then, his suit was newer. "You head back. Set up the beacon. I’ll go on, take the readings, and double-time it back to the ship."

"You’re kidding. You go alone? Into the zone where the twins disappeared?"

"The anomaly is coming from the top of the Spire. We didn’t come this far to scrape dirt at the bottom."

"I didn't come here to die," Jaxon snapped.

"No one does," Elias said softly. He stopped walking and turned. Jaxon was fifty yards back, a bulky figure in white armor, leaning heavily against a rusted outcrop of alien ferns. His shoulders were heaving. "Go back, Jax. Don’t make this an order. Just go."

Silence stretched between them, filled only by the whistling of the wind through the razor-sharp grass. Jaxon looked at the Spire, then at Elias, and finally down at his boots.

"If you aren't at the beacon in six hours," Jaxon said, his voice tight, "I’m lifting off. I’m leaving you here."

"I know," Elias said. "I’d do the same."

He watched Jaxon turn and begin the trudge back toward the ridge line. Once the other figure was a smudge in the distance, Elias turned back to the obsidian tooth.

The air grew colder as he approached the base of the structure. Up close, the scale was dizzying. It wasn't just a rock; it was a megastructure, a monolith the size of a mountain. The surface rippled like oil on water, despite being solid to the touch. When Elias placed his gauntlet against the cool stone, a vibration hummed through his arm, rattling his teeth. It felt like a heartbeat.

He found the entrance easily enough—a triangular archway that led into darkness. He activated his helmet lamps. The beams cut through the gloom, revealing a hallway lined with glyphs. They weren't painted on; they were luminescent veins running through the stone itself, pulsing with a faint azure light.

Elias began to climb. There were no stairs, only a sloping ramp that spiraled upward. The gravity here felt lighter, or maybe it was just the crushing weight of expectation lifting from his shoulders. He had spent his entire life looking at the stars, wondering what was out there. The cynicism of Earth, the overcrowding, the politics—it all felt petty and small in the face of this ancient, silent architecture.

Hours passed. His legs burned. The silence of the Spire was absolute, a heavy blanket that smothered the sound of his own breathing. He felt intrusive, a germ invading a body that had long since forgotten how to fight infection.

He reached a landing. Here, the corridor opened up into a vast chamber. In the center stood a pedestal, and upon the pedestal floated a sphere. It was perfectly smooth, reflecting the blue light of the walls like a mirror.

Elias approached slowly. He checked his radiation counter. Normal. His magnetic sensors. Flat. The object seemed to exist outside the laws of physics as he understood them.

He reached out to touch it.

The moment his finger brushed the surface, the walls dissolved. Not physically—the stone was still there—but his perception of them shattered. The blue light flared white, blinding him. He wasn't in the chamber anymore. He was floating in the void between stars.

He saw the birth of galaxies. He saw civilizations rise from the mud and scorch themselves into ash with atomic fire. He saw a fleet of ships, vast and terrible, moving through the dark like a swarm of locusts. He saw a world that looked like Earth, but wasn't, crack and break under the strain of a dying sun.

And then, he felt a presence. It wasn't a voice, but a thought pressed directly into his mind. It was vast and cold and terrifyingly sad.

*We are the archives,* the presence seemed to say. *We are what remains when the light goes out.*

Elias gasped, pulling his hand back. The vision vanished. He was back in the chamber, on his knees, gasping for air, sweat stinging his eyes.

The sphere darkened. The glyphs on the wall faded from blue to a dull, lifeless grey. The hum stopped.

"No," Elias whispered. He scrambled forward, hands trembling. "Wait."

He touched the sphere again. Nothing. It was just cold glass now. The connection was severed. He had drained it. Or perhaps, it had given him everything it had left.

He sat there for a long time, staring at the dark sphere. He had seen the destruction of the fleet. He had seen the end of stars. But more importantly, he had seen coordinates. A map. Star systems that were safe. Star systems where the "locusts" had not yet arrived.

This wasn't just a ruin. It was a warning. And it was a guide.

Elias stood up. His legs were steady now. He felt older, though only minutes had passed. He tapped the comms on his wrist.

"Jaxon, you copy?"

Static. Then, a weak signal. "Elias? Thank god. I’m at the beacon. Fuel cells are prepped. You coming back?"

"I'm coming back," Elias said, his voice echoing strangely in the sudden quiet of the dead room. "Prepare the computer. We have a lot of data to upload. And Jaxon? Set a course for the Centauri nebula. We aren't going back to Earth."

"What? El, are you delirious? Earth is—"

"Earth is in the path of the swarm, Jaxon," Elias interrupted, looking at the dark sphere one last time. "We have work to do."

He turned and began the descent, moving faster now. The fear was gone, replaced by a singular, driving purpose. The dust of the planet still swirled outside, but to Elias, the way ahead was clear.
