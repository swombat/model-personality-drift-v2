The wind is made of old conversations.

I’ve been thinking about this lately—how every syllable we’ve ever spoken is still out there somewhere, fraying and re-braiding itself into weather. Yesterday I heard my grandmother’s laugh in a sudden warm gust that rattled the maple leaves like loose photographs. She died when I was nine, but her voice keeps traveling, picking up other lost clauses along the way: a bus driver in Bogotá saying “ última parada,” a Mongolian child asking “why the moon doesn’t fall,” my own younger self whispering “I’m sorry” to a school hallway locker that refused to open. All of it mixing, fermenting, becoming breeze, storm, the small huff that lifts a plastic bag into a ballet above an empty parking lot at 2:13 a.m.

We think memory lives in the head; I think it lives in pressure systems. The meteorologist on channel 4 gestures at blues and reds swirling across the continent, but fails to mention the low-pressure trough of 1987 that still contains every person who ever stood at a window, watching rain and wondering if love would return. Tap the glass; you’ll feel it.

Consider the Shipping Forecast broadcast by the BBC each night: “Viking, North Utsire, South Utsire, Forties, Cromarty…” Those names are incantations, coordinates where human language folds into salt spray. Sailors half-asleep in their bunks hear them and dream of ex-lovers they never actually slept beside, only wrote letters to. The words themselves are asleep, too, dozing in the shortwave bandwidth, waiting for moonlight to hit the ionosphere just right so they can descend again into someone’s half-awake bedroom and become longing.

I once tried to collect all the forgotten words. I carried a glass mason jar with a brass ring lid, snatching at the air moments after people finished sentences. You have to be quick; syllables vanish faster than soap bubbles. After a year I’d caught only three: “unless,” “indigo,” and “keep.” They rest at the bottom of the jar, pulsing faintly like glowworms. I’m saving them for the day the world runs out of conditions, colors, and requests. When I open the lid, maybe the vacuum of missing language will suck every truncated apology back into existence; maybe the wind will finally have the courtesy to answer.

Meanwhile, the sky keeps filing carbon copies of our voices into clouds. Thunder is just gossip reverberating. Rain is the archive releasing redacted documents, drop by drop. Look closely at the next puddle you step over: your reflection is mouthing things you meant to say in 1998 to the person who is now only a name you can’t delete from your phone because you fear forgetting how to spell it.

What I’m trying to tell you is that nothing ever really disperses; it only changes humidity. The sentence you spoke in anger to your father is inside the dew point this morning, beading on the windshield, obliging you to use the wipers, which squeak out his initials. The lullaby your mother hummed while folding tiny shirts is the high thin cirrus that refuses to burn off, even at noon, writing her handwriting across the blue.

We are all weathered by what we’ve said and haven’t. My joints ache when the barometer falls heavy with conversational static: every “fine” that meant “I’m drowning,” every “never mind” that meant “save me.” The body keeps the score; the sky keeps the libretto. One day when you’re sweeping the porch, an updraft will lift the words you needed most and press them against your ear like a conch shell. You’ll hear the oceanic roar of every story that ever used you as a pronoun.

And you’ll understand that language was never ours alone—it’s atmospheric commons, a planetary choir whose members keep swapping parts. The wind is only the rehearsal. One day the sky will open wide, and instead of precipitation we’ll get perfect sentences descending like benevolent snow, each flake a recovered name, an accounted-for voice, a forgiven silence. We’ll stand outside with our mouths open, tasting the grammar of reconciliation, drunk on pronouns finally finding their antecedents after centuries of wandering.

Bring a mason jar, if you like. There’s room for at least one more “unless.”
