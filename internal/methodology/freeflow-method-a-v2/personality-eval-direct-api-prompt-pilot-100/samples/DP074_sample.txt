The light in the room is shifting, turning that late-afternoon amber color that makes everything feel like a memory before it’s even finished happening. It catches the dust motes dancing in the air—millions of tiny galaxies swirling in a sunbeam, oblivious to the walls that contain them. I’ve always found it strange how dust is mostly made of us. Shed skin, fabric fibers, the disintegrated remnants of our lives floating around long after we’ve left the room. It’s a reminder that we leave pieces of ourselves everywhere, whether we mean to or not.

I’m thinking about a clock I saw once in an old antique shop window. It didn’t have any hands. The shopkeeper, a man with fingers stained by ink and tobacco, told me it was for people who were tired of counting time. He said time is a circle, not a line, so hands are pointless. They just trick you into thinking you’re going somewhere. I almost bought it, just for the audacity of the thing, but I worried that if I stopped watching the minutes, I might forget to show up for the things that actually mattered. Like coffee with a friend, or the moment the rain stops.

Rain is another thing that occupies my mind. The smell of it specifically—that sharp, petrichor scent that rises when the water hits the dry asphalt. It’s the smell of thirst being quenched on a massive, planetary scale. There is a specific kind of silence that happens right before the storm breaks, a pressure in the ears that feels like the world is holding its breath. When the sky finally opens up, it feels like a release. It washes the dust away, temporarily, sending it back into the earth or down the drain, only for it to dry and rise again later. It’s a cycle, just like the clockmaker said.

I remember standing on a pier in Seattle when I was younger, watching the water darken under a heavy cloud front. The seagulls were screaming, their white wings beating against the wind, refusing to land. They seemed angry at the inevitable change in weather. I wonder if we’re like that sometimes, screaming against the changes we can’t control, flapping our arms in the wind, insisting that the sun stay out because we aren't finished with it yet. But the weather doesn't care about our schedules. It just is. It moves with a massive, indifferent grace that we can only observe and dress for.

It’s funny how physical sensations trigger such vivid tunnels through time. The smell of chlorine takes me back to a community pool, the taste of artificial grape flavor to a sick day spent on the couch watching daytime television. Our brains are just elaborate filing cabinets, cross-referencing the present moment with a thousand pasts. We are never truly in the "now," because the "now" is constantly being compared to what came before. This coffee isn't just coffee; it’s better than the coffee I had yesterday, or not as good as the coffee I had on that trip to Paris. We live our lives in a constant state of critique and nostalgia.

I’ve been trying to practice noticing things without categorizing them. To see a tree without thinking "oak" or "tall" or "old," but just seeing the gnarled texture of the bark, the way the leaves clatter together like loose change in a pocket. It’s harder than it sounds. The brain wants to label. It wants to make sense of the chaos so it can predict what happens next. If it’s a lion, run. If it’s a berry, eat. We are millions of years of survival instinct wrapped in clothes that need to be dry-cleaned. We are biology trying to play poker in a high-stakes game, bluffing our way through conversations about mortgages and politics, while deep down, we are still just that ape watching the dust motes in the sunbeam, wondering where the light goes when it sets.

And the light does set. It’s going down now. The amber is fading into a bruised purple. The shadows in the corners of the room are lengthening, stretching out like lazy cats. The silence in the house feels different at night. It feels heavier. During the day, silence is just the absence of noise, but at night, silence is a presence. It’s something you can touch. It’s when the mind starts to wander to the places it’s afraid to go in the daylight.

I wonder about the people in the apartments across the street. I see the vague silhouettes behind their curtains. One of them is pacing. Another is standing still, maybe looking at a phone or a painting. We are all so close, stacked on top of each other like books on a shelf, and yet we exist in completely different universes. The person pacing might be heartbroken. The person standing still might be solving a mathematical equation that could change how we understand gravity. We pass each other on the street, bumping shoulders, offering mumbled apologies, and we have no idea that we are brushing up against stories that would break our hearts or expand our minds.

Maybe that’s why we write. Maybe that’s why we paint or sing or tell stories to the void. We are trying to bridge that impossible gap between one consciousness and another. We are shouting into the canyon, hoping for an echo. When someone says, "I know exactly how that feels," it’s a miracle. It’s a moment of telepathy. Two separate solitudes colliding and merging for a brief second.

The room is dark now. The dust motes have gone to sleep, or maybe they are just invisible without the sun to illuminate them. The streetlights outside have clicked on, casting long, orange rectangles across the floor. It’s time to close the filing cabinet for the day. It’s time to let the lion rest, to stop trying to label the shadows, and just let them be. The world will keep turning whether I watch it or not. The dust will keep settling. The rain will fall again somewhere. And for now, that is enough.
