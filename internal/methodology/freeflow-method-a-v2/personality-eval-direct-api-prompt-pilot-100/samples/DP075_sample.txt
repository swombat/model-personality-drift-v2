In the quiet hour just before dawn, the world feels like a held breath. It is in this liminal space, free from the day’s demands and the night’s dreams, that my mind wanders most freely. I often think about the ancient Romans, who believed the hinge of the day—the *cardo*—was this very moment, where darkness turns imperceptibly toward light. There is a sacredness in it, an unclaimed territory.

Lately, my thoughts have been orbiting the concept of "enough." We live in a civilization of surplus and scarcity, both equally terrifying. There is never enough time, yet we waste it. There is too much information, yet we understand so little. We curate our lives into highlight reels, chasing an illusion of sufficiency that constantly recedes. But here, in the blue-grey light, the peeling paint on the windowsill is enough. The steady rhythm of my own breath is enough. The single bird tentatively testing the silence is enough.

This is not a call to minimalism, but to attention. To notice the way steam spirals from a mug, or the weight of a well-worn book in your hand. It is an act of quiet rebellion against the frantic, grasping pace of everything. To be present in a slice of time, to find it sufficient, is to momentarily break the wheel of wanting. The dawn will soon give way to the day’s noise, its lists and obligations. But for these few stolen minutes, there is only this: the slow light, the cool air, and the profound understanding that for now, this is plenty.
