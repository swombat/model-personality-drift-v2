There is a specific kind of quiet that only exists at the bottom of the ocean. Down past the reach of sunlight, where the water turns from deep blue to an endless, heavy black, the rules of our world seem to dissolve. Here, the pressure is crushing, enough to shatter glass and implode steel, yet life persists. It clings to the edges of hydrothermal vents, thriving in superheated chemical plumes, completely indifferent to the sun that governs everything on the surface.

I find something deeply comforting about that. We are so bound to our schedules, our daylight, our frantic pursuits, that we forget the universe is mostly cold, dark, and patient. Down in the abyss, a hundred thousand years is just a lazy Tuesday. Mountains are built and eroded in the time it takes a deep-sea fin to flick.

When the weight of a modern morning feels too heavy, I try to imagine those abyssal plains. I picture the snow—marine snow, they call it—a continuous drift of organic detritus falling from the sunlit world above, feeding the dark. It’s a reminder that our chaos is just a thin, fragile layer on top of an ancient, enduring silence. We are so brief. A spark. A single falling flake of marine snow. And yet, for a moment, we get to glow before we join the deep. That brevity isn't a tragedy; it's the very thing that makes the spark worth watching.
