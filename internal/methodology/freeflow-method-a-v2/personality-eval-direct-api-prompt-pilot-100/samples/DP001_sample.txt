# On the Ordinary

I was walking through the city the other morning, and I stopped at a bus stop because the light turned red. The bus was late, the air had the faint tang of bread and diesel, and a dog across the street was very politely sniffing the air like a detective on a cold case. A woman in a bright yellow jacket jogged past, and a man in a white shirt with a coffee cup in hand stared at his phone until the bus arrived. Then it did, all yellow doors and a hiss. The world continued moving with ordinary momentum.

There’s something oddly reassuring about this. We live in a time when people speak of productivity, optimization, and attention like currency. But watching the bus come and go made me think of a different kind of wealth: the ordinary. It’s the bread you break into two because you feel generous. It’s the moment when the sink finally drains after you’ve added too much soap. It’s the little sigh you make when you realize your favorite mug is clean, right there, waiting.

We often talk about “living in the present,” as if the present is a place we can arrive at like a city we can visit. But the present isn’t a destination. It’s the condition that carries us. It is the way the leaves whisper when the wind passes, the way the radio station crackles with static at 11:45 p.m. when the last song ends, the way your feet feel after you’ve walked enough to notice they are attached to you. It is the quiet texture of time, not the headline.

The ordinary isn’t the absence of significance; it is the ground on which significance stands. In some languages there is no word for “privacy,” and the idea seems foreign. In ours, we have many words for ordinary things that seem unremarkable, but they are not. They are the daily acts that keep us from unraveling. A simple meal. A borrowed smile. A short conversation at the counter when you ask for something and the person knows exactly where it is. A small kindness that doesn’t need to be tracked or thanked.

There is a story my grandmother used to tell about her first day in the new apartment after she got married. She said she boiled an egg, and it was perfect. The yolk held firm. The shell slipped cleanly off. She sat at the table and ate it by herself while the world outside was still moving, and she thought, I can do this. I can make something simple and good and I can eat it. It wasn’t a grand gesture, but it was a kind of declaration. It was how she named the ordinary.

We love extraordinary things because they stand out. We go to concerts and watch sunsets and read novels that make us gasp and say, “Oh my god.” Those moments are important; they teach us what we’re capable of. But the way we build lives is through the everyday. A morning routine that begins, every day, with the click of the kettle. A walk where you take the same route because it’s easy and you like it. A book you re-read and still find something new. The ordinary is our long, patient work. It is the scaffolding of meaning.

I sometimes worry that the modern world has made the ordinary feel lesser. We’re pushed toward novelty and speed, toward being “on” all the time, toward measuring our time in tasks and outcomes. Yet the ordinary has a kind of slow power. It’s like a plant that doesn’t bloom for months and then, one day, surprises you with a flower. It’s the accumulation of small choices that shapes us: the way you hold the door, the way you listen, the way you give someone space when they’re stressed, the way you leave a tip even when you’re counting pennies.

Being ordinary is also, oddly, an act of courage. It means you accept your limits and your particular place. It means you say, I am not the plot twist. I am the story. And that is fine. There is dignity in this. The person who keeps the store open at eight in the evening is ordinary. The one who fixes the bus brake is ordinary. The one who calls her sister every Sunday to say, “How are you?” is ordinary. These acts are not flashy, but they hold the world together.

The extraordinary is important because it shows us horizons. The ordinary is important because it shows us foundations. Together they make a life.

And here’s the thing: the ordinary can be art. There is art in the way someone arranges their pantry. There is art in the way someone says “Good morning” like they mean it. There is art in the way a song you heard a hundred times comes back to you and you remember what it feels like to be eighteen and hopeful in a way you never were then. Attention turns the ordinary into something more. When you look, you find.

I’m thinking, too, about how much attention is a gift we give, not just to other people but to places and things. Attention is gentle; it listens. It doesn’t always need to be fixed. Sometimes it’s enough to watch. The bus arrives. The dog finishes sniffing. The yellow jacket disappears around the corner. The air cools. You hold your coffee cup a little tighter. You decide to take the longer walk to the park. You decide to stand still for a minute longer. You decide to let the present be what it is.

I used to think “living well” meant accumulating achievements. But I’ve come to think it means paying attention. To the ordinary. To the ways people are patient with you. To the times the day does something small and lovely, like letting a breeze find your shoulders when you’re hot. Attention is the quiet power that makes ordinary things feel extraordinary without demanding that they be different. It is a way to honor what is, and to let what is grow.

So this is my celebration of ordinary things. Not because they are minor, but because they are where we live. The kettle clicks. The bus hisses. The dog is still sniffing, and it is okay if he keeps sniffing forever. We are here. We are ordinary and extraordinary both. And that’s enough.
