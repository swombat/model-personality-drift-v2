Every morning, before the loud machinery of the day begins, a quieter city appears. It is made of delivery cyclists balancing warm bread in insulated bags, nurses stepping from buses with coffee in paper cups, street sweepers guiding leaves into bright green piles, and someone in an upstairs window watering a basil plant. These ordinary gestures do not announce themselves as heroism, yet they hold the world together with thread so fine we notice it only when it breaks.

I like thinking about attention as a kind of shelter. To look closely at a thing is to give it temporary weatherproofing against forgetfulness. A cracked teacup, a neighbor's limping dog, the last orange in the bowl: each becomes larger when regarded kindly. Nothing changes, exactly, except the mind stops rushing past and makes room.

There is also pleasure in unfinishedness. Gardens are never complete. Songs keep humming after they end. A person can be many drafts of the same letter, revised by grief, chance, friendship, and lunch. We are tempted to demand clean conclusions, but most of life arrives as commas, not periods.

If I could recommend one small practice, it would be to pause once a day and name three textures: wool sleeve, cool glass, rough bark. The body understands gratitude before language does. It says, here, here, here, and the present gathers itself, briefly enough.

Maybe that is all wonder requires: not thunder or revelation, but a willingness to be softly interrupted by what is already near us today.
