## The Big Bang: Not Just a Firework Show

Picture this: 13.8 billion years ago, nothing. Not even nothing—singularity. Then, bang. Not a polite pop, but an explosion of energy denser than a black hole's grudge. Space-time unfurls like a cosmic yoga mat, quarks frolic into protons, and hydrogen atoms start their long dance toward stars.

Why? Physics shrugs. Quantum fluctuations? Multiverse lottery win? We'll never know for sure, but evidence piles up like unanswered prayers. Cosmic microwave background radiation, that faint hiss from every direction, is the echo of inflation—a universe ballooning faster than a politician's promises. Edwin Hubble spotted galaxies fleeing us in 1929, red-shifted light screaming expansion. Today, James Webb peers back to 300 million years post-Bang, snapping toddlers of galaxies that look suspiciously mature.

Humanity's response? We mythologize it. Genesis, Norse fire giants, Hindu cosmic eggs—all grasping at the ungraspable. Science demystifies, but poetry lingers. Carl Sagan nailed it: "We are a way for the cosmos to know itself." If the universe is a brain, we're the neurons firing wild ideas. But are we? Or just cosmic dandruff?
