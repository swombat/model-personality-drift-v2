The page is blank, and the mind, for a moment, is just as white. Then the first word falls like a drop of ink in water, and the ripples begin. Writing freely is an act of trust—trust in the meandering river of thought, in the strange alleys the brain will wander down if you let it. There is no map. The sentence becomes a path cut through tall grass, and you are both the hiker and the landscape. Sometimes, a memory surfaces: the smell of rain on hot pavement, a fragment of a song heard through a neighbor’s window years ago, the exact shade of blue of a childhood bicycle. Why that memory? The mind doesn’t explain. It simply offers, and the hand records.

There is a peculiar intimacy to unstructured prose. You discover what you think not by planning, but by watching your own words appear. You might start with a teacup and end at the rings of Saturn, or begin with a heavy sadness and suddenly find yourself describing the defiant joy of a dandelion pushing through a crack in the asphalt. The connections are emotional, not logical—they rhyme instead of reason. And in that rhyme, a deeper honesty emerges. You are not building an argument but holding a conversation with the part of yourself that speaks in images and echoes. The word count becomes a gentle container, a shoreline that gives the ocean of thought a shape. You write until the tide gently touches that line, and then you look back at what the waves have left behind: a glistening fragment of a larger, inner sea, now made visible on the sand.
