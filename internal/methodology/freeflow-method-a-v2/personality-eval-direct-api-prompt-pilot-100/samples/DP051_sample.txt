At the edge of a small town that didn’t bother to name its streets properly—just Elm, Main, and Other—there was a pond that reflected whatever the sky felt like giving it. Some days it was a sheet of hammered tin, dull and stubborn. Other days it turned into a pane of impossible blue, so clean it looked like it had been installed overnight by careful hands. If you stood at the right angle, you could convince yourself the pond was the sky and the sky was just a rumor.

A woman named Irena used to walk there every morning before the town had fully decided to wake up. She liked the hour when lights were still on in kitchen windows but no one was talking yet. It felt like the world had taken a breath and hadn’t exhaled. She’d bring a small notebook, the kind with a soft cover that curled at the corners, and a pen that sometimes worked.

Irena didn’t write about anything in particular. She wrote around things, like someone sketching the outline of a shape they couldn’t quite see. One page might have a list of birds she’d spotted, except the birds were described as if they were ideas: “a quick thought with wings,” “a mistake that learned to sing,” “a memory that refuses to land.” Another page might be a description of the pond’s surface that ended up sounding suspiciously like a person she once knew.

On a Tuesday that couldn’t decide whether it was still spring or already summer, Irena noticed something new. Near the far edge of the pond, where reeds gathered like conspirators, there was a chair. It wasn’t a grand chair—no velvet, no carved arms—just a simple wooden thing, the kind you’d expect to find in a kitchen that had seen better conversations. It sat half in the water, as if it had walked there and lost its nerve.

Irena stopped on the path. The town wasn’t the kind of place where objects appeared without explanation. Things arrived with stories attached, even if the stories were wrong. A chair didn’t just end up in a pond. It belonged to someone, or had belonged to someone, and now it was here.

She wrote: “A chair at the edge of a decision.”

Then she closed the notebook and stepped closer.

The ground near the reeds was soft, a little treacherous. Each step asked a question: are you sure? are you sure? The chair didn’t move. It had a patient look about it, like it had been waiting longer than anyone realized. One leg was submerged, the water curling around it in small, persistent gestures.

“Hello,” Irena said, because it felt rude not to.

The chair, being a chair, did not respond. But the pond shifted, just slightly, as if something underneath had turned over in its sleep.

Irena crouched and touched the backrest. The wood was cool and smooth, worn in places by hands that were no longer present. She imagined those hands: someone who sat often, who leaned back and stared at a wall or a window, someone who might have been thinking about leaving or staying or neither.

She pulled the chair gently. It resisted at first, the submerged leg reluctant to let go of the mud’s quiet grip. Then, with a soft, reluctant sound, it came free. Water dripped from it in thin lines, returning to the pond without complaint.

Irena set the chair on the grass. It looked different there, less like a question and more like an answer waiting for the right problem.

She sat.

The first thing she noticed was the way the world rearranged itself around her. Sitting in that particular chair, in that particular place, made the pond seem closer, as if it had leaned in. The town behind her felt farther away, its noises softened, its edges blurred. The sky, reflected in the water, seemed to tilt.

Irena opened her notebook again.

“A chair that changes the distance between things,” she wrote.

She stayed there longer than she intended. The sun climbed, the town woke, and somewhere a dog decided it had something important to say about all of it. A couple walked by on the path, glanced at Irena, and then at the chair, as if trying to remember whether it had always been there. They didn’t ask questions. In this town, you learned to let certain things remain unexplained, like a courtesy you extended to reality.

When Irena finally stood, she felt a small reluctance, not in her body but in the space she was leaving behind. The chair remained, solid and unbothered. It didn’t ask her to stay. It didn’t promise anything if she returned.

She walked back toward the path, then paused and looked over her shoulder.

“Don’t go anywhere,” she said, immediately aware of how unnecessary that was.

The chair did not move.

Over the next few days, Irena returned to the pond with a new kind of attention. She wasn’t just observing anymore; she was checking. The chair became a fixed point in her mornings, a quiet certainty. Some days she sat in it, letting her thoughts stretch out in unfamiliar directions. Other days she left it alone, as if it needed its own time to think.

Her notebook changed. The entries grew less like descriptions and more like conversations, though only one side was visible.

“Why here?” she wrote.

“Because you noticed,” she answered, though she wasn’t sure if that was her or something else.

“What happens if I move you?” she asked on another day, her pen pressing a little harder into the page.

“Then I would be somewhere else,” came the reply, simple and unhelpful.

One morning, after a night of rain that tapped insistently on windows and roofs, Irena arrived to find the pond fuller, its edges stretched. The reeds bowed slightly, and the path was slick. The chair was gone.

She stood very still, as if movement might bring it back or confirm its absence in a way she wasn’t ready for. The place where it had been was marked only by a faint disturbance in the grass, a memory of weight.

Irena walked to the edge of the pond, scanning the surface. No sign of wood or legs or anything that suggested a chair had ever been there. The water reflected a sky that had already decided to be bright, as if nothing had happened.

She opened her notebook.

“A chair that leaves without saying goodbye,” she wrote.

For a moment, she considered closing the notebook and going home, letting the morning collapse into something ordinary. But the absence of the chair felt like a kind of presence, too—an outline where something had been, a shape that asked to be filled.

Irena sat on the grass where the chair had stood. It was damp, and she could feel the coolness through her clothes. The world did not rearrange itself in the same way. The pond stayed at a distance that felt more honest, less inclined to lean in.

“Did I imagine you?” she wrote.

The page stayed quiet.

A breeze moved across the water, breaking the reflection into fragments. For a second, it looked like a thousand small skies had been scattered across the surface, each one complete and separate.

Irena closed the notebook and watched as the ripples settled, the fragments finding their way back into a single, continuous image. The sky reassembled itself without effort, as if it had always known how.

“Maybe that’s all it is,” she said, not writing this time. “Things appear. Things leave. The space they make is real, even after they’re gone.”

She stood, brushed the damp grass from her hands, and took one last look at the place where the chair had been. It was just grass now, doing its best to look like it had never held anything at all.

On her way back through the town that was finally awake and making noise about it, Irena felt the outline of the chair in her mind, as clear as if she were still sitting in it. The way it had changed the distance between things lingered, a subtle shift in how the world arranged itself around her.

At a corner where Elm met Other, she paused and looked at the street signs, their paint slightly chipped, their names still insufficient.

“Some things don’t need to stay,” she said, to no one in particular. “They just need to happen.”

And then she kept walking, the morning unfolding ahead of her, ordinary and strange in exactly the same measure.
